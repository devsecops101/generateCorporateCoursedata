So, what exactly is automation bias? In simple terms, it is the tendency we have to trust automated systems a little too much. When a machine gives us an answer, we often just accept it without stopping to question whether it is actually correct. And this is not just a quirk or a bad habit. It is a well-documented cognitive bias, meaning it is a predictable pattern in how human beings think. What happens is that once a system has made a decision for us, we naturally reduce our own critical thinking. We let the machine do the heavy lifting, and we stop double-checking. Now, this has been a recognised issue in fields like medicine, aviation, and finance for quite some time. But here is the thing: as AI becomes part of everyday working life for more and more of us, this bias is growing rapidly. The machine feels reliable, so we switch off. And that is where the risk begins.

Script 3 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------

One of the trickiest things about AI is that it doesn't really know when it's wrong. It delivers incorrect information in exactly the same confident, polished tone as when it's completely right. There's no hesitation, no "I'm not sure about this one." It just sounds authoritative. And that's where the danger creeps in. Because when something sounds certain, we naturally tend to trust it. We stop questioning it. We skip the double-check we might otherwise do. So errors that would normally get caught, a wrong figure, a misattributed fact, a flawed recommendation, they slide straight through our review process unnoticed. And before long, those mistakes aren't just sitting in a draft document. They're influencing real decisions, being shared with colleagues, or going out to customers. The AI didn't flag a problem, so we assumed there wasn't one. That assumption is exactly what we need to start challenging.

Let's look at some real-world examples, because automation bias isn't just a theoretical concern. Think about sat-nav systems. There are well-documented cases of drivers following directions straight into closed roads, simply because the device said so. Or consider doctors who accept an AI diagnostic suggestion without actually checking whether it matches the patient's symptoms. In recruitment, hiring managers sometimes reject candidates purely because an algorithm gave them a low score, without ever questioning why. Pilots, too, can fall into the habit of trusting autopilot so completely that their own manual skills and situational awareness start to fade. And we've all probably approved a spell-checker suggestion without actually reading the sentence properly. What these examples share is the same underlying pattern: a person had the ability to catch an error, but the presence of an automated system made them less likely to do so. That's automation bias in action, and it shows up everywhere.

Script 7 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How do we actually rebuild confidence in our own thinking after we've been leaning on AI a little too heavily? The key is to start small. Begin making everyday, low risk decisions without immediately reaching for an AI tool. Notice when your instincts turn out to be correct, because they will. It helps to keep a short log, just a few notes, recording moments when your own judgement spotted something the AI got wrong or missed entirely. That kind of evidence is genuinely reassuring. Another useful habit is to say your reasoning out loud before you check any AI output. It forces you to commit to your own thinking first, rather than letting the AI do the heavy lifting from the start. And please be patient with yourself here. Trust in your own judgement does not come back overnight. It rebuilds gradually, one small decision at a time, and that is perfectly normal.

Script 8 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring everything together with a few key points worth keeping in mind as you go forward. First and most importantly, AI gets things wrong. It can sound completely confident and still be mistaken, so always take a moment to verify what it tells you before you act on it. That means staying alert and thinking critically, rather than simply accepting the output at face value. Your own judgement is not something to be set aside just because a machine has offered an answer. In fact, if something an AI produces surprises you, or feels a little off, that instinct is worth listening to. Pause, question it, and look a bit closer. And as a general habit, build in a review step before you do anything based on AI output. These are simple practices, but they make a real difference. The goal is not to distrust AI entirely, just to work with it wisely.
