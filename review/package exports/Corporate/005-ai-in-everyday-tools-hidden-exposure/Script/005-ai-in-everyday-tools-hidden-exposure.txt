Script 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Welcome to this lesson on AI in everyday tools. When most people think about artificial intelligence, they imagine something futuristic or complicated, something they have consciously chosen to use. But here's the reality: AI is already woven into the tools you use every single day, and most of the time, it's not signposted at all. Your email client, your search engine, your spreadsheet software, even your smartphone keyboard—all of these have AI quietly working away in the background. There's no big announcement, no flashing label saying 'AI is here.' It's simply built in. And because it feels so familiar and ordinary, most of us don't stop to think about what that actually means. The risks that come with using AI-powered tools often go completely unnoticed, precisely because we don't realise we're using AI in the first place.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start by looking at where AI is already showing up in the tools you probably use every single day. When you open your email and a suggested reply appears at the bottom of the screen, that's AI. When your word processor quietly fixes a spelling mistake or finishes a sentence for you, that's AI as well. Even in something as familiar as a spreadsheet, you might notice it offering to complete a pattern or suggest a formula. Type something into a search bar and watch it predict what you're about to ask—again, that's AI working behind the scenes. And if you've ever been in an online meeting where a transcript appeared automatically, yes, AI was listening and converting speech to text in real time. The point here is that AI isn't some distant, futuristic technology. It's already woven into the everyday tools sitting right in front of you.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now let's talk about what actually happens when these AI features do their work. Those helpful suggestions aren't generated locally on your device—they require your data to travel somewhere else entirely. When your email suggests how to finish a sentence, those words may be going to an external server for processing. Your grammar checker isn't just spotting typos; it's often uploading your entire document to the cloud to analyse it. And here's something that surprises many people: if you're using a free productivity tool, your content may be helping to train the AI models that power these features. Your actual words, from real documents and real emails, become part of the learning process. The convenience is real, but so is the journey your data takes to provide it.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Here's what catches most people off guard: you probably never chose to use these AI features in the first place. Most AI capabilities are switched on by default, buried in privacy settings you may never have looked at. When you install an app or accept an update, you're often agreeing to terms that give broad permission for AI processing. The assumption is that you want these smart features, and opting out requires you to actively hunt through settings menus. Updates frequently add new AI capabilities without clearly announcing them, and the terms of service are written to cover whatever AI features the company might develop in future. You're not necessarily making a conscious decision to use AI—you're simply failing to refuse it.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's look at some specific examples you'll recognise. In Microsoft Outlook or Gmail, those auto-complete suggestions are powered by cloud-based AI that processes your email content. PowerPoint's design suggestions come from AI services that analyse your slides and content. If you use Grammarly, it's reading everything you type across all your applications and storing it for analysis. Calendar tools like Calendly don't just schedule meetings—they're analysing your availability patterns, contact relationships, and meeting history. Even your company's internal search tools likely use AI algorithms to decide which documents appear first in your results. These aren't futuristic concepts; they're features you probably used this week without realising it.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how can you start recognising AI in your daily workflow? Look for features that anticipate what you're going to do next—auto-suggestions, predictive text, or smart recommendations. Pay attention when tools seem to 'learn' your preferences over time or work seamlessly across different devices, as this usually requires cloud processing. Notice features that offer contextual help, like suggesting the right template or recommending people to invite to a meeting. And here's a simple test: if a feature stops working when you're offline, it's probably using AI services that require an internet connection. Once you start looking for these patterns, you'll begin to see just how pervasive AI has become in your everyday tools.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now that we understand where AI is hiding, let's talk about practical steps you can take to reduce your exposure. Start by spending a few minutes reviewing the privacy settings in your most-used apps—you might be surprised by what's enabled by default. If there are AI features you don't need or use, turn them off. There's no reason to leave them running in the background. Be thoughtful about what information you type into any tool, especially sensitive details like client information or confidential business data. When apps send update notifications, take a moment to read what's changed, because AI features are often added quietly in updates. And wherever possible, stick to software your organisation has officially approved, as these tools are more likely to have been reviewed with your security and privacy requirements in mind.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's wrap up with the key points worth remembering. First, AI isn't always obvious—it's quietly built into tools you use every day, so you may be interacting with it without realising. Second, when you use these tools, your data could be travelling to external servers, so it's worth being mindful of what you're sharing. Make checking your privacy settings a regular habit, because they can change with updates. Before typing anything sensitive, pause and consider whether that information should really be processed by an AI system. And remember, you don't have to navigate this alone—your IT team is there to help answer questions and provide guidance on approved tools. These are simple habits, but they can make a real difference in protecting your organisation's information.
