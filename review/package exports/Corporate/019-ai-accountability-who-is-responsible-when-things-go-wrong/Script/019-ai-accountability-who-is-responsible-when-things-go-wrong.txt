Welcome, and thank you for joining this course on AI accountability. Whether you realise it or not, artificial intelligence is already influencing decisions in many workplaces, from hiring and scheduling to customer service and beyond. And as AI becomes more embedded in how we work, a really important question starts to emerge: when something goes wrong, who is actually responsible? That is exactly what this course is designed to help you understand. We will look at how responsibility is shared across different roles, whether you are a frontline employee, a manager, or part of senior leadership. The good news is that you do not need any prior knowledge of AI to follow along. Everything is explained in plain, practical terms. By the end, you will have a much clearer sense of where your own responsibilities lie and what that means in your day-to-day role.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So when we talk about AI accountability, one of the first questions people ask is, "Well, who exactly is responsible?" The answer is that responsibility flows through three main levels in any organization. At the ground level, employees who use AI tools day to day are responsible for using them properly and flagging anything that seems wrong. Managers sit in the middle, overseeing how AI influences decisions within their teams and ensuring proper procedures are followed. At the top, leaders set the policies and standards that govern AI use across the entire organization. But here's the key point: accountability isn't dumped on just one person or level. It's genuinely shared, with each level playing a distinct but interconnected role.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now, when we step back and look at the bigger picture, AI accountability actually involves more than just the people inside your organization. Yes, you have your internal stakeholders - employees, managers, and leaders - but there are also external players with important roles. The vendors who build and supply AI tools carry responsibility for how those systems behave. And regulators sit above everything, setting standards and holding organizations accountable when things go wrong. When an AI system causes problems, the response often requires coordination across all these different groups. It's an ecosystem approach to responsibility, where multiple layers provide oversight and enforcement.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
If you're an employee who uses AI tools in your daily work, your responsibilities might seem straightforward, but they're genuinely important. First and foremost, you need to follow your organization's AI policies - not just the spirit of them, but the actual guidelines. You should only use AI tools for purposes that have been approved, and if you encounter outputs that seem unusual or concerning, report them. Part of being a responsible AI user is staying aware of when and how AI is influencing your work, rather than treating it as a black box. And when you're not sure about the right approach, ask for guidance. These might seem like small responsibilities, but they're the foundation of responsible AI use.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
For managers, the accountability picture becomes more complex because you're responsible not just for your own AI use, but for your entire team's. That starts with making sure everyone actually understands the organization's AI policies - not just that they've been told about them, but that they genuinely know how to apply them. You need to review AI-assisted work before it gets approved or moves forward, because you're accountable for the outcomes your team produces. Good managers also monitor how AI tools are being used day to day, provide clear guidance about which tools are appropriate for which tasks, and ultimately take responsibility when AI-related problems occur within their area of supervision.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
At the leadership level, AI accountability takes on a governance dimension. Leaders are responsible for creating the framework within which everyone else operates. That means establishing comprehensive policies that actually make sense for how work gets done, defining clear boundaries around acceptable AI use, and creating proper procedures for reporting and escalating concerns. Leadership also needs to ensure people have the training and resources they need to use AI responsibly, and to keep the organization compliant with whatever regulations apply. Essentially, leaders create the conditions that make responsible AI use possible throughout the organization.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When something feels off with an AI system, the most important thing you can do is act early. Don't wait and hope the problem resolves itself. Start by raising it with your immediate supervisor, because they can often address issues quickly at a local level. If your organization has formal reporting channels - like an ethics hotline or AI governance process - use them. And if you raise a concern and nothing happens, don't let it drop. Escalate it further up the chain. One practical habit that really helps is documenting what you observed, when you noticed it, and what you did about it. That documentation can be valuable if the issue gets investigated later. Remember, you don't need to be a senior leader or technical expert to speak up about AI concerns.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's bring everything together with five core principles you can carry forward. First, when AI makes a mistake, a human being needs to own that and put it right - systems don't fix themselves. Second, be absolutely clear about your own role in any AI-driven process - where does your responsibility begin and end? Third, if something feels off or you're unsure about a decision, say so. Raising concerns early is always better than staying silent. Fourth, document your AI-related decisions. This protects you, your team, and the people your work affects. Finally, remember that accountability isn't something that sits with a policy document or a system - it starts with each of us, individually, in the choices we make every day.
