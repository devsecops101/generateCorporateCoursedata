Welcome, and thank you for joining this course. AI tools have become a regular part of working life for many of us, whether we realise it or not. They help us draft emails, summarise information, respond to customers, and much more. But when those tools come into contact with customer data, we need to be thoughtful about how we use them. Customer information is sensitive, and even a small slip, like pasting the wrong details into a prompt, can lead to a serious privacy breach. The good news is that protecting customer data does not have to be complicated. This course is designed to give you straightforward, practical guardrails that you can apply in your day-to-day work, starting from today. You do not need any technical background or specialist knowledge to follow along. Everything here is built around real situations you are likely to encounter. So let us get started and look at how to use AI responsibly and confidently.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So why are we talking about this now? Well, the truth is, AI tools have quietly become part of everyday working life. They're built into the software your team already uses, things like email, spreadsheets, and document editors. And here's the thing: your staff are probably already using them, whether your organisation has a policy in place or not. That's not a criticism, it's just the reality of how quickly this technology has moved. The problem is that when people use these tools, they're often sharing customer data without even realising it. A quick copy and paste into a chatbot, a prompt that includes a client's name or contact details, and suddenly that information has left your control. Regulators are watching this space very closely, and the rules around data protection haven't gone away just because the technology is new. So the risks are genuine, and getting to grips with this sooner rather than later is really important.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Before we look at how to use AI tools responsibly, we need to be clear about what actually counts as sensitive customer data, because it is broader than most people initially think. The obvious stuff includes things like a customer's name, their home address, or their phone number. But it also covers financial details, so anything related to bank accounts or payment cards. Health and medical information is particularly sensitive and is treated very carefully under data protection law. Then there are login credentials, usernames and passwords, which should never be shared with or entered into an AI tool under any circumstances. And finally, do not overlook behavioural data, things like what a customer has previously bought or their stated preferences. All of these categories tell us something personal and private about an individual, and that is exactly why they need to be handled with care. Keeping this list in mind will help you make better decisions as we work through the rest of this course.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Before you paste any customer data into an AI tool, you need to strip out anything that could identify a real person. Start with the obvious stuff, names and contact details like email addresses and phone numbers. Those should be the first things to go. Next, if your data includes any kind of customer ID or account reference, swap those out for neutral codes so there is no way to trace them back to an individual. Then take a moment to look at dates and locations, because sometimes those details on their own can be enough to identify someone, especially if the dataset is small. Once you have worked through all of that, you are in a much safer position to share the data with an AI tool. Think of it as a quick checklist you run through every single time. It only takes a few minutes, but it makes an enormous difference to how safely you are handling your customers' information.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now let's look at what this means in your everyday workflow. When you're testing AI features or setting something up, always use fictional examples rather than real customer data. There's simply no need to risk actual information when placeholder data will do the job just as well. Before you share any customer information with a third-party AI service, take a moment to check your organisation's policy, because not all tools will be approved for that kind of use. A helpful way to think about AI input fields is to imagine they're public noticeboards. You wouldn't pin a customer's personal details up there, so don't type them in either. And here's a practical tip: keep a simple log of what types of data you've used with AI tools. If there's ever an audit or a question later on, you'll be really glad you kept that record.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The key to handling customer data safely with AI is making protection automatic, not something you have to remember each time. Develop a standard routine for cleaning data before you use it, and stick to that routine every time. When you replace IDs with placeholder codes, use a consistent system so you can track things if needed. Always take a second look for identifiers you might have missed the first time, things like email signatures or references buried in text. If you're setting up a new process, test your anonymisation approach with sample data first to make sure it's working properly. The goal is to build habits that protect customer information without slowing down your work. When data protection becomes automatic, you can focus on getting the best results from AI tools while keeping everyone safe.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's talk about some warning signs that should make you stop and think before you carry on using an AI tool with customer data. First, if a tool is asking for an entire customer database just to complete a simple task, that's a problem. You should only ever share the minimum data needed. Similarly, if real customer names start appearing in the output when you never expected them to, that tells you the tool may be handling data in ways you haven't fully understood. Watch out for tools that give customers no way to opt out of having their data used, because that's likely a compliance issue straight away. Be cautious too if the tool is automatically saving everything you type into it, as your inputs could be stored and used for purposes you're not aware of. And finally, if the privacy policy is vague, confusing, or simply doesn't exist, treat that as a serious red flag. These are the moments where you need to pause and ask questions before going any further.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring everything together. The single most important habit to build is this: never put real customer information into an AI tool unless you are absolutely certain it is approved and secure. Always anonymise your data first, then work with it. And before you do anything, check your organisation's policy, because the rules will vary from one workplace to another. A simple phrase worth keeping in mind is 'if in doubt, leave it out.' It really is that straightforward. Think of AI tools the same way you would think of any other third-party software you might use at work, and apply the same level of care around data handling. Finally, there is no need to rush. Start with something small, see how it goes, build your confidence, and then gradually do more. Getting this right from the beginning will save you a great deal of trouble further down the line.
