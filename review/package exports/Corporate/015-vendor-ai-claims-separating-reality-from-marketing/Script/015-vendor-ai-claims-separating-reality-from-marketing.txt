Welcome, and thanks for joining this course. If you've spent any time looking at software or technology solutions recently, you'll have noticed that "AI" seems to appear in just about every vendor's marketing materials. It's on the brochures, the websites, the sales decks. But here's the thing: bold claims and actual capability are not always the same thing, and that gap can be costly if you don't know what to look for. Not every product labelled "AI-powered" is doing something genuinely intelligent, and some are doing far less than the marketing suggests. This course is designed to help you cut through that noise. We'll give you practical, straightforward tools to ask the right questions, spot the red flags, and make more confident decisions when vendors come to you with their latest pitch.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start by pulling back the curtain on what vendors actually mean when they use AI terminology. When a product is described as "AI-powered," that phrase can mean almost anything. In many cases, it simply refers to basic automated rules or keyword matching, nothing remotely sophisticated. Vendors rarely tell you which type of AI they're actually using, because the terminology is vague enough that they don't have to. Words like "intelligent" and "smart" are marketing choices, not technical descriptions. There's no agreed industry standard that defines what qualifies as AI in a product, so anyone can use the label freely. What you'll often find is that the loudest, boldest claims appear precisely where the actual evidence is weakest.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Understanding how these claims get crafted helps explain why they're so often misleading. The wording is driven almost entirely by marketing teams rather than technical ones. The goal is to sound impressive and modern, not to be precise. Instead of explaining what the technology actually does, vendors reach for buzzwords that do heavy lifting without saying much. This creates a dangerous dynamic: vague language leaves gaps, and we naturally fill those gaps with our own assumptions. We hear "AI-powered" and imagine something far more sophisticated than what might actually be running behind the scenes. This is exactly the pattern we need to recognise and guard against.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do we actually put vendor claims to the test? The most reliable approach starts with standardised benchmarks. These are structured tests that measure AI performance consistently, so you can compare systems on a level playing field. But benchmarks alone aren't enough. Look for independent audits carried out by organisations with no financial interest in making a product look good. Can the performance results be reproduced by third parties? Are there credible reviews from researchers or industry analysts who have examined the system independently? If impressive figures only appear in vendor marketing materials and nobody else can replicate them, that's a significant warning sign.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When you're in conversation with vendors, here are practical ways to cut through polished pitches. First, ask for live demonstrations using real data, not pre-recorded videos where everything goes perfectly. Push them on accuracy claims. Numbers like "95% accurate" mean nothing without knowing how that figure was measured and under what conditions. Ask whether the AI was trained on data from your industry, because systems built for one sector often behave differently in another. And here's a question that catches many vendors off guard: who is accountable when the system makes a mistake? These questions reveal far more than any sales presentation.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now let's look at the warning signs that should make you slow down and ask harder questions. Be immediately sceptical of any claim that a system is "100% accurate" or "fully autonomous" because no AI system is, period. If vendors can't tell you error rates or system limitations, that's a problem. Watch for inability to explain what their AI actually does beyond vague terminology. If they can't point to real-world examples or independent testing, you're essentially taking their word for everything. And be very cautious about guaranteed results, because AI operates in complex, unpredictable environments where responsible vendors don't make such promises.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Beyond obvious red flags, watch for subtler patterns in how AI products are oversold. Case studies that show only cherry-picked outcomes are a common tactic. Be wary of guaranteed results despite AI's inherent uncertainty, and pay attention to evasive answers when you ask about failure scenarios. Many vendors rely heavily on terminology that sounds impressive but carries no real meaning. If they're reluctant to provide technical details or explain their testing methodology, that reluctance itself tells you something important. And if claims seem too good to be true for the price point, they probably are.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's wrap up with a framework you can apply to any AI evaluation. Always demand real evidence, not just marketing materials or vendor-created case studies. Test systems in your own environment before making commitments, because what works for one organisation may not work for yours. Treat vague claims as red flags that require deeper investigation. Push consistently for specific, measurable results with clear methodology behind them. And throughout the process, maintain healthy skepticism. Being skeptical doesn't mean being negative, it means being smart. This approach will serve you well every time someone promises their AI will transform your business.
