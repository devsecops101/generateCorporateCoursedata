One of the trickiest things about working with AI tools is that they sound incredibly confident, even when they are wrong. And I mean subtly wrong, not obviously wrong. The language is smooth, the sentences are well-formed, and the tone feels authoritative. But fluent writing is not the same as accurate writing. AI can get facts slightly off, misquote a statistic, give you the wrong date, or attribute something to a source that never actually said it. And here is the real danger: the more polished the output looks, the easier it is to just trust it and move on without checking. We naturally associate confident, well-written language with reliability, and AI exploits that instinct without meaning to. So throughout this course, we are going to look at exactly how these errors show up, why they are so easy to miss, and what you can do to catch them before they cause a problem.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, how do you actually spot when AI has got something wrong? The tricky part is that it rarely sounds wrong. In fact, it often sounds impressively confident, which is one of your first warning signs. If the tone feels almost too assured, too polished, that is worth noticing. You might also find that sources are mentioned in a vague, hand-wavy sort of way, with no real specifics you can actually check. And watch out for answers that seem suspiciously perfect, almost too neat and tidy for a genuinely complex question. Another subtle clue is when details are nearly right but not quite, like a name that is slightly off, or a date that does not quite match. And then there are claims that sound entirely plausible but that you simply cannot verify anywhere. None of these signs on their own means the AI is wrong, but together they are telling you to slow down and dig a little deeper.

One of the trickiest things about AI-generated content is how convincing it sounds, even when it is wrong. The writing is often fluent, well-structured, and confident in tone, and that polish can easily fool us into assuming the facts are solid too. But here is the problem: AI does not flag its own mistakes. There are no warning signs, no hesitation, no little asterisk saying "please double-check this." A wrong date or a misattributed quote just sits there, nestled inside an otherwise perfectly reasonable paragraph. And because the surrounding text feels familiar and plausible, our brains tend to accept the whole thing without question. We naturally trust writing that looks and sounds professional. That is exactly what makes these errors so dangerous. They do not stand out. They blend in. So as we move through this course, we are going to look at how to spot the mistakes that hide in plain sight.

Script 7 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, AI can sound incredibly convincing, but sounding right and being right are two very different things. The good news is that fact-checking doesn't have to be complicated. Start by cross-checking anything important against a source you already trust, like a reputable news outlet, an official website, or an academic reference. Then search for the original claim yourself rather than just accepting the AI's version of it. Pay close attention to dates as well, because context matters enormously. Something that was true five years ago might not be true today. It's also worth asking where the information actually comes from. Did a real expert say it? Is there a credible source behind it? And if you're looking at statistics or figures, always try to verify those independently, because numbers are particularly easy to misquote or misrepresent. These are simple habits, but they make a real difference to how confidently and safely you can use AI-generated content.

Script 8 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring this all together. The most important thing to hold onto is that AI doesn't always fail in obvious ways. It can sound completely authoritative while quietly getting things wrong, and those subtle errors are the ones that catch people out. A slightly wrong date, a misattributed quote, a statistic that's just a little off. These things slip through precisely because they sound so reasonable. The good news is that you already have what you need to catch them: your own judgement. And a quick check against a trusted source really does take no more than a few seconds. That small habit can save you from some genuinely embarrassing or costly mistakes. If you want to put this into practice straight away, just pick one piece of AI-generated content this week and fact-check it. That single step will start building the kind of confident, critical approach that makes AI a genuinely useful tool rather than a liability.
