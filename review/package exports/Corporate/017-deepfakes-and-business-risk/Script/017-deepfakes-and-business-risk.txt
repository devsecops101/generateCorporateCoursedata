Welcome to this course on deepfakes and business risk. Now, you might be wondering what exactly a deepfake is, and why it matters to your business. Put simply, deepfakes use artificial intelligence to create convincing fake versions of real people. We are talking about fabricated voices, faces, and even video footage that can look and sound remarkably genuine. And this is not just a concern for celebrities or politicians. Businesses like yours are increasingly being targeted. Fraudsters are using this technology to impersonate executives, manipulate staff, and trick organisations into handing over money or sensitive information. The scale of this fraud is growing rapidly, and many businesses are simply not prepared for it. So throughout this course, we are going to look at how these threats work, how to spot them, and most importantly, what practical steps you can take to protect your organisation. Let us get started.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's start with the basics. What actually is a deepfake? At its core, a deepfake is a piece of AI-generated video or audio where someone's face or voice has been swapped out and replaced with someone else's. The technology behind it uses machine learning, which means a system has been trained on large amounts of existing footage and recordings until it can convincingly mimic a real person. And when I say convincingly, I mean convincingly enough to fool colleagues, clients, and even some security systems. Now, you might be thinking this sounds like something that requires a serious technical background or expensive kit. But that is really no longer the case. The tools to create deepfakes are widely available, often free, and all you need is a standard laptop. That shift is really important, because it means the barrier to creating this kind of content has dropped dramatically, and that has significant implications for businesses of all sizes.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's look at how criminals are actually using this technology against businesses. The most common attack is CEO fraud, where someone creates a fake video call that appears to show your chief executive authorising a large wire transfer. Voice cloning is equally dangerous, with fraudsters calling finance teams using a perfect replica of a senior manager's voice. We've also seen cases where fake conference calls have been used to manipulate stock prices by spreading false information that appears to come from company leadership. Customer-facing attacks are growing too, with fake customer service representatives extracting sensitive data from unsuspecting clients. And increasingly, criminals are impersonating suppliers or vendors, redirecting legitimate payments to their own accounts. What makes these attacks particularly dangerous is that they exploit our natural trust in familiar faces and voices.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do you actually spot a deepfake? There are a few tell-tale signs worth knowing about. Start with the face itself. Deepfakes often struggle to replicate natural human movement, so you might notice the person barely blinks, or their facial expressions look a little stiff and limited. Look closely at the edges of the face and hairline too, as these areas can appear blurry or seem to shift slightly as the person moves. Then pay attention to the audio. If the voice feels even slightly out of step with the lip movements, that is a red flag. Lighting is another clue, particularly if it looks inconsistent or just a bit off. Skin can also appear unusually smooth or almost waxy, lacking the texture you would expect. And finally, glance at the background. Warping, blurring, or repeating patterns in the background are a strong indicator that something has been artificially generated or manipulated.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Strong organisational policies are your first line of defence against deepfake fraud. Start by establishing clear escalation procedures for any financial requests, especially those that come through video calls or unusual channels. No matter how convincing someone appears, transfers above a certain threshold should always require dual approval from verified sources. Think carefully about your digital footprint too. The less audio and video material of your senior executives that's publicly available, the harder it becomes for criminals to create convincing fakes. Consider implementing written confirmation requirements for sensitive decisions, so there's always a paper trail that can be verified later. And review your access controls to ensure that no single person, regardless of their apparent seniority, can authorise major financial or data decisions without proper verification.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When someone contacts you with an unexpected request, your verification process is critical. Always confirm through a completely separate channel, whether that's a phone call to a known number or an email to a verified address. Never use contact details provided in the original request. Code word systems work particularly well for high-risk communications, giving you an instant way to confirm someone's identity. Don't be afraid to ask specific questions that only the real person would know about recent conversations or company details. If someone calls you claiming to be a colleague, suggest switching to a video call so you can see them properly. And remember, always use predetermined callback numbers that you've verified in advance, not numbers that someone provides during a suspicious conversation.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Your staff are your strongest asset in preventing deepfake fraud, but only if they know what to look for. Regular training sessions should cover the latest threats and show realistic examples of what these attacks actually look like. Practice scenarios are particularly valuable because they help people recognise the pressure tactics that fraudsters use. Make sure everyone knows exactly who to contact if they encounter something suspicious, and emphasise that there's no penalty for reporting a false alarm. One of the most important habits to build is simply slowing down when faced with urgent requests. Fraudsters rely on creating panic and time pressure to bypass people's better judgement. Keep your training updated too, because this technology is evolving rapidly and new attack methods are emerging all the time.

So let's bring everything together with the points that really matter. At the heart of this is one simple truth: deepfakes are designed to make you believe you're seeing or hearing a real person, when you're not. And here's the thing, nobody is off limits. Whether you're a senior leader or someone just starting out, you could be a target. So what do you do about it? You build a habit of verification. Before you trust something, check it. Before you act on an instruction, pause and ask yourself whether this feels right. That moment of hesitation could save your organisation from a serious mistake. And if something does seem suspicious, don't sit on it. Report it straight away to the right people in your business. The sooner it's flagged, the sooner it can be dealt with. These aren't complicated steps, but they are powerful ones. Keep them in mind every single day.
