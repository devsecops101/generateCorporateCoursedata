Script 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start with the basics. AI data leakage is when an AI system reveals sensitive information that it really should not be sharing. This doesn't necessarily mean someone has hacked into a system or done something malicious. It can happen in surprisingly ordinary ways. When AI tools are trained or given instructions, they sometimes absorb private information along the way, and that information can unexpectedly surface in responses. We're talking about things like someone's name, medical details, financial records, or confidential business information appearing in outputs that were never meant to contain it. What makes this particularly concerning is that most people have no idea the AI even had access to that information in the first place. And here's the part that often surprises people: you don't need to be a sophisticated attacker to trigger this. Sometimes, simply asking a question in a certain way is enough to cause the AI to reveal something it should have kept private.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Understanding why data leakage matters goes beyond the immediate moment when you receive an AI response. The real concern is what happens to that information afterwards. AI responses are frequently logged and stored, meaning they can be read by system administrators, third-party providers, or even other users in some cases. So the risk isn't just about what you see on your screen in that moment. It's about where that information ends up afterwards. Most people wouldn't even spot when leakage is happening because the output just looks like a helpful answer. But that seemingly innocent response containing leaked data could now be sitting in logs, backups, or shared systems where it never should have been. This hidden reach is exactly why awareness of data leakage is so critical in corporate environments.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Data leakage comes from two main sources that can work independently or combine to create problems. The first source is the training data itself - AI systems learn from enormous amounts of information, and sometimes that includes sensitive details that later resurface in unexpected ways. The second source is user inputs - when people type confidential information into AI systems, they're directly feeding it into the mix. What makes this particularly concerning is that repeated or similar inputs increase the likelihood of sensitive information slipping out later. The key point here is that none of this requires malicious intent from anyone. Data leakage can happen simply as a consequence of how these systems work, which is exactly why building awareness and good practices is so important for everyone using AI tools in the workplace.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's think about where the risk actually begins, and that's right at the start, during training. When an AI system is built, it learns from enormous amounts of data, and sometimes that data contains sensitive or private information. Nobody necessarily planned for that to happen, but it does. The concerning part is that this information doesn't simply disappear once training is complete. It can resurface later in the AI's responses if someone asks the right kind of question. We're talking about personal details, internal documents, or confidential records that were part of the training datasets. You might assume there's some automatic filter that catches sensitive content before it becomes a problem, but there really isn't. Even data that's been anonymised isn't necessarily safe, because if the patterns within it are distinctive enough, it's still possible to identify individuals or organisations from what the AI produces.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's look at some realistic scenarios that show how data leakage can happen in everyday workplace situations. Picture someone asking an AI chatbot a follow-up question, and the system repeats back salary information from an earlier part of the same conversation. Or imagine an AI assistant that inappropriately recalls and mentions a colleague's medical condition from a previous session. These aren't far-fetched examples. We also see cases where AI systems accidentally expose confidential client names when generating suggestions, or where outputs include private login credentials that were stored somewhere in the system's accessible data. Sometimes details about restricted internal projects find their way into responses that were never meant to contain them. Each of these scenarios demonstrates how AI systems can reveal sensitive information without any malicious intent, simply because they don't inherently know what they shouldn't share.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Looking at real incidents across different industries helps us understand the breadth of this issue. In corporate settings, AI writing tools have casually suggested colleague names or job titles in documents, simply because they encountered that information during training. Email systems with AI features have exposed previous recipients through inappropriate autocomplete suggestions. We've seen AI-generated business reports accidentally include salary figures or budget details that were never meant to be shared. Healthcare organisations have encountered AI summarisation tools that surfaced sensitive patient medical information inappropriately. In software development, AI coding assistants have occasionally included real passwords or API keys in their code suggestions, pulled from examples they were trained on. These aren't theoretical risks - they're genuine incidents that have caught organisations off guard, which is exactly why proper understanding of this topic is so important.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So what can you actually do to reduce the risk of data leakage when using AI tools? Building a few simple habits makes a significant difference. First, avoid putting real names or personal details into your prompts. If you're testing something, use made-up or dummy data instead of genuine information about customers or colleagues. Before sharing any AI-generated output with others, take a moment to read through it carefully - sometimes the AI includes unexpected details from earlier in the conversation. Try to keep your prompts as general as possible rather than including sensitive specifics. The less confidential information you include, the less there is to potentially leak. Finally, stay alert to anything in the output that surprises you or seems out of place. These practical habits, built into your everyday use of AI tools, are one of the most effective ways to keep sensitive information protected.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's wrap up with key principles to carry with you in your daily AI use. Before pasting anything into an AI tool, pause and ask yourself whether that information is sensitive. Once you have an output, treat it like you would an email - something that could easily be shared or seen by others. A simple rule of thumb: if you're not sure whether something is safe to include, leave it out. Before sharing any AI-generated content, give it a quick review, particularly if your original prompt contained personal or confidential details. Remember that your organisation's data protection policies apply to AI tools just as much as they do to traditional systems like email or file storage. Finally, here's a practical step for this week: look back at one recent AI interaction and evaluate whether anything sensitive was included. It's an excellent way to start building these protective habits from the ground up.
