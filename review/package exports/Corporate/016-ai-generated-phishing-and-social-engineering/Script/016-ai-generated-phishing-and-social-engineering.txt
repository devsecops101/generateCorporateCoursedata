Welcome, and thanks for joining this course on AI-generated phishing and social engineering. Over the next little while, we're going to look at one of the fastest-growing threats in the online world right now. Criminals are using artificial intelligence to create scam messages, fake voices, and convincing impersonations that can fool almost anyone. And the scary part is, these fakes have become so polished and realistic that even experienced, tech-savvy people are getting caught out. This isn't just a problem for big companies or IT professionals either. Ordinary people, at home and at work, are being targeted every single day. The good news is that once you know what to look for, you can protect yourself and the people around you. By the end of this course, you'll have the knowledge and confidence to spot these scams before they cause any real harm. So let's get started.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, what exactly is AI phishing? At its core, it's a type of scam where criminals use artificial intelligence to create and send fraudulent messages, but on a massive scale and with unprecedented sophistication. The goal remains the same as traditional phishing: trick you into clicking malicious links, giving away passwords, or transferring money. What makes AI phishing so dangerous is how it leverages machine learning to craft messages that feel genuinely legitimate. These systems can analyse vast amounts of data about potential targets and generate personalised content that's far more convincing than the old template-based scams we used to see.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Here's what makes AI phishing particularly insidious: the personalisation. AI systems can scrape information from social media, professional networks, public databases, and even data breaches to build detailed profiles of their targets. They'll use your real name, job title, company details, and recent activities to craft messages that feel like they're coming from someone who genuinely knows you. This isn't just adding your name to a form letter. AI can reference a purchase you made last week, mention a project you're working on, or even mimic the communication style of your colleagues. This level of personalisation makes people drop their guard because the message feels authentic and relevant to their lives.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do you spot these AI-generated attacks? There are several warning signs to watch for. First, be suspicious of any message creating urgency, especially around financial matters or account access. AI-generated content often has suspiciously perfect grammar and spelling, so polished writing is no longer automatically trustworthy. Pay attention to the overall tone as well. If something sounds like it's from your boss or IT team but feels slightly off in some way you can't quite pinpoint, trust that instinct. Scammers also love to pressure you into acting immediately without giving you time to think or verify the request.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's look at some technical warning signs you can check before taking any action. Always examine sender email addresses carefully, because they often contain subtle differences from legitimate addresses. Be wary of messages that use generic greetings like 'Dear Customer' despite containing personal details about you. No legitimate organisation will ever ask for passwords or login credentials via email. Before clicking any links, hover over them to see where they actually lead. And be particularly cautious about messages that arrive suspiciously soon after you've made a purchase or interacted with a service online.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
To help you recognise these attacks in the wild, let's look at some common scenarios. You might receive a perfectly crafted email warning that your account will be suspended unless you verify your details immediately. Or perhaps a message appearing to come from your manager asking you to process an urgent payment while they're in meetings. IT security alerts claiming your password has been compromised are another favourite. Fake invoice confirmations with malicious attachments, or bogus customer service follow-ups after legitimate transactions, are also increasingly common. In each case, the AI crafts content that feels timely, relevant, and urgent.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, we've talked about how sophisticated these AI-generated scams can be, and now let's focus on what you can actually do to protect yourself. The first thing is simply to slow down. Before you click any link, just pause for a moment and ask yourself whether you were expecting this message. If something feels off about who sent it, don't rely on the email address alone. Go and verify it independently, perhaps by calling the organisation directly or visiting their official website. It's also really worth setting up multi-factor authentication on your accounts, because even if a scammer gets hold of your password, that extra layer of security can stop them in their tracks. Now, this one is important: trust your gut. If something feels wrong, it probably is. And finally, if you do spot a suspicious message, report it. Most email providers and organisations have a straightforward way to do this, and reporting helps protect everyone else too.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring everything together. The core message here is simple: AI has made phishing attacks far more convincing than they used to be. These aren't the badly written scam emails of ten years ago. They're polished, they feel personal, and they're designed to make you panic and act fast. That urgency, that pressure, is deliberate. Attackers want you to click before you think. So the best thing you can do is slow down. If something arrives out of the blue asking you to do something quickly, pause. Verify it through a completely separate channel, not by replying to the same email or clicking a link within it. And remember, no genuine organisation will ever demand you act within seconds or face dire consequences. Most importantly, trust your instincts. If something feels slightly off, even if you can't quite explain why, that feeling is worth listening to. Report it, and let someone else take a look.
