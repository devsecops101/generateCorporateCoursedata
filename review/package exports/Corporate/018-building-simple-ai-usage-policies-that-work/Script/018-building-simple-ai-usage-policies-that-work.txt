Let's start by looking at why most AI usage policies fail, because understanding the problem is the first step to solving it. In my experience, there are five common reasons. First, the policy is simply too long. Nobody reads a forty-page document, full stop. Second, even when people do read it, the language is so vague that staff have no idea what they're actually allowed to do. Third, organisations write the policy and then do absolutely nothing to train their people on it. Fourth, AI tools move incredibly fast, and the written policy gets left behind, becoming outdated almost immediately. And fifth, nobody is clearly responsible for keeping it alive and up to date. When there's no owner, nothing gets maintained. So what we end up with is a document that exists on a shared drive somewhere, that nobody reads, nobody follows, and nobody updates. Sound familiar?

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The fundamental issue with most failed AI policies is that they're written for the organisation, not for the people actually doing the work. They tend to come from legal or IT teams who, with the best intentions, produce something lengthy and full of abstract guidance that nobody quite knows how to apply on a Tuesday afternoon when they're trying to get something done. There are no real examples, no practical scenarios, just rules that feel disconnected from reality. And because staff weren't involved in creating them, the policy lands in an inbox, gets skimmed once, and is never looked at again. There's also often a tone of anxiety running through them, as if AI is something to be feared and controlled rather than used sensibly.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Beyond the structural problems, there's a design issue that kills adoption before it even begins. When policies are packed with legal or technical jargon, they mean very little to the people who are supposed to follow them. They talk about AI in the abstract but completely ignore how staff actually do their jobs day to day. Most importantly, when staff have no input in the creation process, the policy doesn't reflect reality, and it simply doesn't get followed. Instead of feeling supported, people feel like they're being managed by someone who doesn't understand their work. That's not a recipe for compliance.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So what actually goes into a policy that people will use? First, it needs to spell out exactly which AI tools your organisation has approved, so staff aren't left guessing. Alongside that, you want concrete examples of what is and isn't acceptable, because vague rules tend to get ignored. You also need a clear section on handling sensitive or confidential information, because that's where the real risk tends to sit. It's worth naming a specific person or team who owns the policy and can answer questions, so staff know exactly who to go to. Include a review date, because AI is moving quickly and a policy written today will need revisiting before long. These six elements together give you something practical that your team can actually follow day to day.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Building on those components, your policy needs a solid structural framework. Start with a clear statement about the policy's purpose, who it applies to, and what it covers. People should be able to read it and immediately understand why it exists. Then spell out the specific ways AI can be used in your organisation, so there's no guesswork involved. Alongside that, give people clear guidance on what to avoid, whether that's sharing sensitive data or using AI for certain types of decisions. And build in the expectation that the policy will be updated regularly, because your guidance needs to keep pace with how quickly AI is evolving.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the biggest mistakes organisations make when writing AI policies is trying to sound official. They load the document with technical jargon, acronyms, and legal-sounding language, and the result is that nobody actually reads it. So the golden rule here is to write for real people, the actual humans who will be using these tools day to day. If your policy reads like a contract, it will be treated like one, which means it gets filed away and forgotten. Instead, aim for one clear rule per sentence. Keep it short, keep it direct. When guidance is simple and clear, people make fewer mistakes. They understand what's expected, they remember it, and they follow it.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When it comes to practical implementation, there are some non-negotiables that make the difference between a policy that works and one that sits unused. Your policy should be readable in five minutes or less. If someone has to set aside an afternoon to read it, it won't get read. Include real, practical examples for common situations people encounter. Make sure there's guidance on sensitive information handling, and that staff know exactly who to contact with questions. Most importantly, build in a review schedule. AI is moving quickly, and a policy written today will need revisiting before long. These practical elements ensure your policy actually functions in the real world.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's bring everything together. The single most important thing to remember is this: simple works. If your policy is too complicated, people will ignore it. Focus on how you want people to behave, not just on listing which tools they can or cannot use. Start small - you don't need to solve everything on day one. Write something brief, share it with your team early so they feel part of the process, and then update it as you learn more. A one-page document that someone actually reads and follows is worth a hundred times more than a polished, lengthy policy that sits untouched. So here's your practical next step: pick just one AI use case that's already happening in your organisation, and draft three short, clear rules around it this week.
