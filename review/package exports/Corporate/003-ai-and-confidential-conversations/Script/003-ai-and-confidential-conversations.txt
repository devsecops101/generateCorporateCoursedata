Let's start by looking at why this is important, because the risks here are very real. When people use AI tools without thinking carefully, sensitive information can end up outside the organisation entirely, shared with third-party systems that were never meant to see it. Clients can be exposed without anyone realising it happened, which is a serious breach of trust. And here is something many people do not consider: a lot of AI platforms store your conversations, sometimes indefinitely, which means what you typed today could still be sitting on a server somewhere years from now. That creates real problems around compliance, particularly if your organisation operates under regulations like GDPR or professional confidentiality standards. And when things do go wrong, the reputational damage can happen very quickly indeed. A single incident can undermine years of trust with clients and colleagues. So this is not about being fearful of technology; it is about using it responsibly and understanding what is at stake before you start.

Script 3 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Think about what actually gets typed into these AI tools, because it's often more sensitive than people realise. When someone asks an AI to help draft an email, they might include a colleague's name, or mention a meeting that's coming up. When they're summarising a project update, they might paste in internal details that aren't meant to go beyond the team. And it doesn't stop there. People use AI to help with tricky HR conversations, to format financial reports full of figures and targets, or to write communications involving client and customer information. None of this is done with bad intentions. It's just that when a tool is helpful and easy to use, we stop thinking carefully about what we're feeding into it. The goal of this lesson is to help you pause before you type, and ask yourself whether the information you're about to share is actually yours to share in that way.

Script 4 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Think about what AI tools are actually capturing, because it's often more than people are aware. When you use AI in a meeting or a conversation, it's not just picking up the main points. It's recording names, job titles, and anything said in passing, even a brief comment about a sensitive situation. If there's a chat function involved, the full transcript is typically stored, not only a tidy summary. And if you paste a document into an AI tool, or share content directly with it, that content is being processed and potentially retained. There's something else worth knowing too. Some AI solutions are sophisticated enough to pick up on the emotional tone of your language, drawing inferences about context and sentiment from the way you phrase things. So the picture AI builds from a single session can be surprisingly detailed. That's why it's so important to think carefully before you share anything, even something that feels minor in the moment.

Script 5 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
What should you actively avoid sharing when you're using AI tools? Even if a tool feels private or secure, it is really important to treat it with caution. You should never input client names or any details that could identify them. Equally, never type in passwords or login credentials of any kind, even if you think it might help the AI assist you with something. Confidential meeting notes are another area to be careful with, as these often contain sensitive discussions that are not meant to go beyond your organisation. The same goes for personal information about your staff, such as performance issues or health details. And finally, if your business has plans that have not yet been made public, keep those well away from AI tools. The golden rule here is: if you would not share it openly with a stranger, do not share it with an AI.

Script 6 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now that we know what to be cautious about, let's talk about what is perfectly fine to share with AI. General questions are a great starting point, things like asking how to structure a report or what a particular legal term means. Publicly available information is fair game too. You can also use anonymised examples, so if you want AI to help you work through a scenario, just strip out all the names, dates, and any identifying details first. Your own ideas and rough drafts are fine to share, as long as they contain nothing about clients or colleagues. And specific task prompts work really well, for example asking AI to help you improve the tone of a piece of writing. A simple rule of thumb to keep in mind: if you would hesitate to pin that information on a public noticeboard for anyone to read, do not type it into an AI tool. When in doubt, leave it out.

Script 7 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Summarising here are some practical steps you can start using straight away. The most important habit to build is this: pause before you type anything into an AI tool and ask yourself whether what you're about to share is sensitive. Don't wait until after you've hit send. Think of any AI tool like a public microphone in a private room. It might feel contained, but the information can travel further than you expect. Before inputting anything, remove or anonymise names, client details, and any sensitive figures. If you're not sure which AI tools are actually approved for use at work, check with your manager or IT team. That's a really simple step that many people skip. And if you do accidentally share something you shouldn't have, report it to your line manager promptly rather than hoping it goes unnoticed. The core habit is straightforward: pause, review, then proceed. That small moment of reflection can make a very big difference.

Script 8 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
If something is confidential, keep it out of any AI tool you haven't verified is safe to use. Assume that whatever you type could be stored or seen by someone else, because in many cases, it can be. Before you use any AI tool for work purposes, check your organisation's policy first. If you're not sure whether something is appropriate to share, the safest approach is simply not to share it. Think of public AI tools the way you'd think of a public forum or a shared noticeboard. You wouldn't post sensitive client details there, and the same logic applies here. And as a practical first step, I'd encourage you to find and read your workplace's AI usage guidelines this week, if you haven't already. Small, informed habits like these make a real difference.
