Script 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start by acknowledging what AI genuinely cannot do. AI has no emotional intelligence whatsoever. It cannot sense the feeling in a room, read between the lines, or understand what someone is truly experiencing. It also misses crucial human context—the unspoken history, relationships, and cultural nuances that shape every situation. Here's a critical point: AI cannot be held accountable when things go wrong. It cannot own a mistake or face consequences the way a person can. AI also struggles with complex ethical questions where there's no clear right or wrong answer. And finally, real life is messy and layered, but AI tends to see things in black and white terms. These aren't minor gaps—they're fundamental limitations that define where human judgment remains essential.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
There are moments where human presence is simply irreplaceable. Consider the conversations that matter most: supporting someone through grief, delivering devastating medical news, or helping during a personal crisis. These situations aren't just about sharing information accurately—they're about reading emotional cues, adjusting your tone in real time, and making someone feel genuinely heard. AI can process data brilliantly, but it cannot sense the weight in someone's silence or respond to a trembling voice with authentic compassion. Empathy isn't a feature you can program—it comes from lived experience and genuine human connection. When emotions run high and stakes matter most, that's exactly where we need to be thoughtful about AI's role.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When AI makes mistakes, the consequences extend far beyond technical glitches. Consider someone wrongly denied a loan—that's not a minor inconvenience, it can push a family into genuine financial crisis. Think about a patient whose symptoms are misread by an algorithm, leading to wrong treatment that could be life-threatening. We've already seen automated hiring tools screen out perfectly qualified candidates due to biased programming. These aren't theoretical risks—they're documented cases where AI errors have changed lives for the worse. When we discuss AI decision-making failures, we're not talking about abstract performance metrics. We're talking about real moments that can alter someone's future forever. That's precisely why getting these boundaries right matters so much.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
What makes human decision-making fundamentally different? Unlike machines, we actually live with the consequences of our choices, and that personal stake changes how we approach decisions entirely. We bring irreplaceable context to every situation—two scenarios might look identical on paper, but humans recognize the subtle differences that algorithms miss. Our personal values also shape how we prioritize outcomes, and this diversity of perspective is something no AI can replicate. Most importantly, when decisions go wrong, people need someone they can hold accountable. AI cannot stand up, take responsibility, and explain its reasoning the way a human can. This isn't a weakness in human decision-making—it's the foundation of responsible judgment.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
AI's limitations become most apparent in complex, ambiguous situations. Since AI has never lived a human life, it hasn't experienced loss, fear, or joy, which means it completely misses the emotional weight behind many decisions. It also cannot make genuine moral judgments—it doesn't understand why some outcomes carry more significance than others. Cultural context presents another major blind spot. What's appropriate in one community might be deeply offensive in another, and AI often cannot navigate these subtle but crucial differences. When facing genuine uncertainty with no clear patterns to follow, AI tends to produce responses that feel mechanical or even harmful. Understanding these blind spots isn't about dismissing AI—it's about using it appropriately.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Certain decisions demand human control, regardless of how sophisticated AI becomes. Any choice that could fundamentally alter someone's life—their employment, health outcomes, or legal status—requires human judgment at the final stage. These situations often hinge on factors no dataset can capture: the context behind someone's circumstances, the emotional dynamics at play, or insights that come only from lived experience. Ethical dilemmas where reasonable people might disagree especially need human reasoning, where someone can look another person in the eye and explain their decision. AI can provide valuable input and analysis, but it cannot own the moral weight of life-changing choices. Where genuine accountability is required, human judgment isn't just preferred—it's essential.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's be specific about where AI should never have final authority. Employment decisions—hiring and firing—affect someone's livelihood and require understanding of human potential beyond what data reveals. Medical diagnoses demand the intuition and experience that comes from treating real patients, not just analyzing symptoms. Legal sentencing involves moral judgment about justice and proportionality that no algorithm should determine. Child welfare decisions require reading family dynamics and assessing safety in ways that demand human insight. And mental health crises need authentic human connection when someone is at their most vulnerable moment. In all these areas, AI can support professionals by surfacing information or identifying patterns, but the final decision must rest with someone who can be held truly accountable.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's consolidate everything into actionable principles. Think of AI as your most capable research assistant, not your replacement decision-maker. For any choice that significantly impacts people's lives, a human must remain accountable for the final outcome. High-risk decisions involving fairness, safety, or fundamental rights deserve extra layers of human oversight. Never accept AI output uncritically—always verify, question, and apply your own judgment. Remember this rule: the more difficult a decision is to reverse, the more human oversight it requires. Now, rather than keeping this theoretical, I challenge you to identify one specific decision in your work this week where you can apply these principles. Start small, but start somewhere. That's how responsible AI use becomes second nature.
