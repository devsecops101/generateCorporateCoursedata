Script 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start with a question that might feel a little uncomfortable. When a student uses AI to write their essay, and then puts their name on it and submits it, who actually did the learning? The words might look polished, the argument might seem coherent, but if the student didn't wrestle with the ideas themselves, something important has been missed. Learning isn't only about producing a finished piece of work. It happens in the struggle, in the moments where you're not quite sure how to phrase something, or how to structure your argument, and you have to figure it out. That process is where expierience is built. When AI skips that process entirely, those skills simply don't develop. So this lesson is really about exploring that tension. AI can be genuinely useful in education, but there's a real risk that convenience becomes a shortcut, and that shortcut comes at the cost of actual understanding.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
There is a real cost to taking the easy route, and it is not always obvious straight away. When a student uses AI to complete a task for them, they skip the part that actually builds understanding, the bit where you struggle, make mistakes, and figure things out. That struggle feels uncomfortable, but it is where genuine learning happens. Without it, knowledge does not stick, and skills do not develop. The worrying thing is that this happens gradually. Each shortcut feels like a small convenience, but over time those gaps in foundational knowledge start to add up. Critical thinking, writing, problem-solving, these are not skills you can absorb passively. They require practice and effort. So while AI can feel like progress, if it is doing the thinking instead of supporting it, it is quietly working against the learner. Convenience in the short term can mean lost capability in the long term.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So what do we actually mean by the dependency trap? It's simpler than it sounds. When a student uses AI to complete a task without doing any of the thinking themselves, they get the answer, but they miss the learning. the brain genuinely needs to struggle a little in order to build understanding. That productive effort is where real learning happens. When AI removes that effort repeatedly, something quietly starts to erode. Problem-solving skills, critical thinking, the ability to reason through something independently. It doesn't happen overnight, and that's partly what makes it so tricky. By the time anyone notices, the habit is already well established. What's more, a student can start to feel confident based entirely on what the AI produced, rather than what they themselves actually understand. And if you've always borrowed the answer, you never really find out what you know. That's the trap.

Script 7 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do we actually start reclaiming those skills? The first step is before you reach for AI, have a go yourself. Even if you struggle, that struggle is where the learning happens. And here is something that might surprise you: making mistakes on purpose is genuinely useful. When you push yourself into uncomfortable territory, you discover exactly where your gaps are. Once you have had a try, check your own work before seeking any help. Ask yourself, does this make sense? Is this right? That self-checking habit builds real confidence over time. The goal here is not to find shortcuts, it is to build consistent habits that stick. Think of it like going to the gym. One session does not transform you, but showing up regularly absolutely does. And please, celebrate the small wins along the way. Finished a task unaided? That matters. Noticed an error yourself? Brilliant. Those little moments add up to something significant.

Script 8 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The core message of this lesson is that AI, as brilliant as it is, can actually get in the way of real learning if we rely on it too heavily. When things feel difficult, that struggle is not a sign that something is going wrong. It is actually where the learning happens, where genuine experiences are built. So the invitation here is to shift how you think about AI. Use it as a tool to support your thinking, not as a shortcut that replaces it. Before you turn to AI for help, ask yourself whether you actually understand what you are working on. Have a go at the task yourself first. Even if your first attempt is messy or uncertain, that effort matters enormously. AI works best when you bring something to the conversation, not when you hand the whole problem over. Keep that in mind as you move forward, and you will get so much more out of both your learning and the technology itself.
