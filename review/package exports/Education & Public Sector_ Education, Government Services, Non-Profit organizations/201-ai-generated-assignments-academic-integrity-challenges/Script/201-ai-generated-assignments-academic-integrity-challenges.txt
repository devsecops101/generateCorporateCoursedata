Welcome to this course on AI-generated assignments and academic integrity. This is a topic that has moved very quickly from something theoretical to something educators are dealing with right now, every single day. Over the past several years, AI writing tools have become remarkably easy to access, and students at every level, from secondary school through to postgraduate study, are using them. That means the work being submitted today might have been written entirely by a machine, or perhaps just partly so. The tricky part is that the plagiarism detection software most institutions rely on was never designed to catch this kind of content, so a lot of it simply slips through. Meanwhile, policies and guidelines are struggling to catch up with how fast the technology is moving. So throughout this course, we are going to explore what this all means for educators, institutions, and students, and think about how we might respond in a thoughtful and practical way.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Just a few years ago, AI writing tools were a curiosity, something tech enthusiasts experimented with. But since around 2022, that changed dramatically. These tools are now free, widely available, and require no technical knowledge whatsoever. A student can open a browser, type in a question, and have a full, structured essay in front of them within seconds. And this isn't limited to one subject area. Whether it's a history essay, a biology report, or a literature analysis, these tools can handle it all. For many students, using AI has simply become a normal part of how they study and complete their work, much like using a search engine or a spell checker. The challenge is that educational institutions were largely caught off guard by how fast this all happened. Policies, assessments, and guidance simply haven't kept pace with the speed of adoption. That's what we're here to explore.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So before we go any further, let's get clear on what we actually mean by AI-generated content, because it's broader than most people initially assume. Yes, it includes a full essay written by ChatGPT and submitted as a student's own work, but it goes well beyond that. It also covers situations where a student takes AI output and makes a few light edits before handing it in, or where they use an AI tool to paraphrase a source, restructure an argument, or summarise a piece of reading. In other words, it's not just about copying and pasting text wholesale. The tricky part is that the boundary between using AI as a helpful writing aid and actually submitting AI-generated work is genuinely blurry, and that's what makes this such a challenging area for educators and institutions. Keeping that complexity in mind will help us make sense of everything else we cover in this course.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's talk about originality, because I think a lot of people assume it simply means not copying someone else's words. But in a world where AI can generate text in seconds, we need to think about this differently. Originality now has to include your thinking process, not just the final words on the page. What did you wrestle with? What decisions did you make? What connections did you draw from your own understanding? Those things matter enormously now. When you sit down to work through a problem, to analyse an argument, or to construct your own response to a question, that effort is actually the point. It is where your learning happens. So if an AI does that thinking for you, the originality is gone, even if the words look perfectly fine. The ideas need to come from you. The thinking is the work. That is what your tutors and institutions are really looking for.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So if AI can produce a polished essay in seconds, what does that mean for how we design assessments? Well, it means we need to think differently. Rather than focusing purely on the finished product, we want to see how students think along the way. Things like oral presentations, live demonstrations, or reflective journals are much harder to outsource to a chatbot, because they require the student to actually show up and engage in the moment. Similarly, tasks that are tied to something specific and local, like analysing an event that happened on your campus last week, are difficult for AI to answer in any meaningful way. Building in checkpoints throughout a project, encouraging peer feedback, and asking students to submit drafts all help reveal genuine understanding over time. And when we set tasks rooted in real experience, students actually have something personal and worthwhile to produce. The goal is to design assessments where doing the work honestly is simply the easier path.

Script 8 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The arrival of AI tools means we genuinely need to rethink how we design assessments, not just patch up the old ones. Alongside that, our understanding of originality has to evolve, because it means something quite different now than it did even a few years ago. It's also worth remembering that detection tools, while useful, are not reliable enough to be the whole answer. Clear, honest policies matter enormously, because when everyone understands the boundaries, students and educators alike are better protected. Rather than simply adding more rules, the real opportunity is to redesign the tasks themselves so that AI assistance becomes less of a shortcut and more of a genuine learning tool. And finally, you don't need to overhaul everything at once. Start with small changes, see what works, and keep adapting as things develop. That's a healthy and realistic approach for any educator right now.
