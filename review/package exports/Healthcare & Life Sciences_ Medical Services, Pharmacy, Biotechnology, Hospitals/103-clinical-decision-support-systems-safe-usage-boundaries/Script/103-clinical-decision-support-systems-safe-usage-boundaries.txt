Script 1 (Slide 1)

Welcome, and thank you for joining this course on clinical decision support systems and their safe usage boundaries. Whether you work in an NHS setting or private practice, you have probably already encountered these AI-powered tools, or you soon will. They are becoming a standard part of clinical life, and that is largely a positive development. But with any new tool comes a critical question, and that question is the entire focus of what we are going to cover together: where should AI assistance stop, and where must human clinical judgement take over? This course is designed specifically for clinicians, nurses, and allied health professionals who are new to working alongside these systems. You do not need any technical background whatsoever. What you will gain is a set of clear, practical boundaries that you can apply straight away in your day-to-day practice. Throughout the course, we will use real-world clinical scenarios to make every concept as grounded and relevant as possible. So let us get started.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start by understanding what AI genuinely cannot do, because this is where safe practice really begins. These tools are built on population-level data, which means they reflect patterns across thousands of patients, but they know nothing specific about the person sitting in front of you right now. They cannot factor in someone's personal values, their anxieties, or the social circumstances shaping their health. And importantly, if something goes wrong, the AI carries no professional or legal responsibility. You do. There is also something irreplaceable about physical presence. A clinician in the room picks up on things no algorithm can—subtle cues, tone, the way someone holds themselves. And finally, training data has gaps. Rare conditions, atypical presentations, underrepresented populations—these are exactly the situations where AI is most likely to let you down. Recognising these limits is not about distrusting the technology. It is about using it wisely.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the most serious limitations we need to address is the quality and completeness of the data that trains these systems. Historical medical data inevitably contains gaps and biases that get embedded into AI recommendations. If certain populations were historically underrepresented in clinical studies, the AI will perform poorly for those groups today. Similarly, if key patient information is missing from the electronic record—perhaps an undocumented allergy or a medication taken years ago—the algorithm has no way of knowing what it doesn't know. It will confidently make recommendations based on incomplete information. This is particularly concerning because AI systems rarely flag their own uncertainty. They present their best guess as if it were definitive, leaving you to recognize when that guess might be dangerously wrong.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Here's where human clinical judgment becomes irreplaceable: AI operates in a world of static data points, but real patients exist in dynamic, changing circumstances. The algorithm cannot see that your patient walked into the room looking unusually anxious, or that they mentioned their spouse is seriously ill, or that they seem confused in a way that wasn't evident in their last visit. These contextual factors can be absolutely critical to safe treatment decisions. Your clinical experience allows you to integrate information that never makes it into the formal record—the hesitation in someone's voice when they say they're taking their medication, or the family member who quietly expresses concern about cognitive changes. AI systems also cannot adapt to your local environment: the particular challenges your patient population faces, the resources available in your setting, or the cultural factors that influence how patients experience and express illness.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
AI systems excel with common conditions that appear frequently in their training data, but they struggle significantly with rare diseases and atypical presentations. When a patient presents with an unusual combination of symptoms, or a rare condition that affects only a few thousand people worldwide, the algorithm simply hasn't seen enough examples to make reliable recommendations. This creates a dangerous situation where AI appears most confident in exactly the scenarios where it should be most cautious. Complex patients with multiple comorbidities also challenge these systems, as the interactions between different conditions and treatments can create scenarios that weren't adequately represented in the training data. Emergency situations present another critical limitation: when rapid clinical decisions are needed, AI processing delays can be life-threatening, and the dynamic nature of emergencies requires human adaptability that algorithms cannot match.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
It's crucial to remember that using AI tools does not transfer your professional responsibility to the machine. You remain fully accountable for every clinical decision, legally and professionally. Medical councils and regulatory bodies expect you to exercise independent clinical judgment, regardless of what technology suggests. Your professional indemnity insurance requires you to document your own reasoning process, not simply to follow algorithmic recommendations. When obtaining patient consent, you must be able to explain risks and benefits based on your clinical assessment—you cannot delegate this responsibility to an AI system. The duty of care you owe to each patient is fundamentally human and cannot be transferred to an algorithm. This means that every AI recommendation must be filtered through your professional judgment, with clear documentation of why you accepted or rejected the system's suggestions.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Learning to recognize when to override AI recommendations is a critical skill for safe practice. If your clinical instinct tells you something is wrong, don't dismiss that feeling simply because an algorithm suggests otherwise. Years of clinical experience have taught you to recognize patterns and warning signs that may not be captured in any dataset. When a patient's presentation doesn't match what the AI expects, investigate further before accepting the system's recommendations. High-stakes decisions—those involving significant risks or irreversible treatments—always require human confirmation, regardless of how confident the AI appears. Vulnerable populations, including children, elderly patients, and those with cognitive impairment, need individualized assessment that goes beyond algorithmic recommendations. And in emergency situations, trust your training and experience to make rapid decisions without waiting for AI processing.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's establish a practical framework for safe AI use in your daily practice. First, always treat AI suggestions as starting points for your own clinical reasoning, not final answers. Compare every recommendation against your clinical assessment and what you observe in the patient before you. Document your thought process clearly—explain why you agreed or disagreed with the AI's suggestions, what additional factors you considered, and how you reached your final decision. Maintain heightened oversight for high-risk patients and complex clinical situations, recognizing that these are precisely the scenarios where AI is most likely to provide inadequate guidance. Familiarize yourself with your organization's policies on clinical decision support systems and know how to escalate concerns about system performance. Finally, seek ongoing training to stay current with both the capabilities and limitations of the AI tools you use. Remember: you are not just a user of these systems—you are the clinical safety net that ensures they enhance rather than compromise patient care.
