Welcome to this course on AI and Patient Data Privacy Risks. Over the next few lessons, we are going to explore something that really matters in modern healthcare, and that is how artificial intelligence interacts with some of the most sensitive information that exists, your patients' health data. AI is becoming a powerful tool across the NHS and healthcare organisations more broadly, but with that power comes genuine responsibility. The risks around data privacy are not theoretical, they are real, and the consequences of getting things wrong can be serious, both for patients and for the organisations involved. There are clear rules and regulations in place that govern how patient data must be handled, and those rules apply just as much when AI is involved as they do in any other context. Crucially, this is not just a concern for IT teams or senior managers. Everyone who works with or around these systems has a part to play in keeping patient data safe.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Before we look at the risks, it's worth being clear about what we actually mean by patient data, because it covers far more than most people realise. Yes, it includes the obvious things like someone's name, date of birth, and NHS number, along with their contact details such as their address, phone number, or email. But it also includes clinical information like notes from appointments, test results, diagnoses, and prescriptions. It goes further still. Photos, voice recordings, and biometric data all count too. And here's something that often surprises people: even seemingly harmless details like appointment times or attendance records are considered patient data. The key principle is this: if a piece of information could be used, on its own or in combination with something else, to identify a specific patient, then it falls under the definition of patient data and must be handled with care. So the scope is genuinely broad, and that matters as we explore how AI fits into all of this.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, what actually goes wrong when AI and patient data aren't properly protected? The risks are very real. If an AI system is accessed without authorisation, sensitive information like diagnoses, medications, and personal details can be exposed. And unlike a single paper record being misplaced, a breach involving an AI tool can affect thousands of patients in one go. There's also a subtler risk that's easy to overlook: even data that's been anonymised can sometimes be re-identified using AI pattern recognition, so removing a name doesn't always guarantee privacy. On top of that, when NHS organisations use third-party AI vendors, there's a chance that patient data is being stored or processed outside of NHS governance controls, which creates real accountability gaps. And once health data is leaked, the consequences for patients can be serious, including discrimination, financial fraud, or being targeted by scams. These aren't just technical problems; they have a direct human impact.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Understanding the legal framework is crucial because it defines your obligations as a healthcare professional. Under UK GDPR, health data receives special protection, and you need explicit patient consent before processing it through AI systems. The Data Protection Act 2018 sets out specific requirements for NHS organisations, and the Information Commissioner's Office has the power to impose substantial fines when things go wrong. But the legal obligations go beyond statute law. There's also a common law duty of confidentiality that applies to all patient information, regardless of how it's processed. Professional bodies like the GMC, NMC, and others have published clear guidance on data handling responsibilities. These aren't just bureaucratic requirements - they exist to maintain the trust that patients place in healthcare professionals and ensure that sensitive information is treated with the respect it deserves.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When it comes to choosing AI tools for healthcare work, your organisation will have an approved list of software that's been properly vetted for security and compliance. This isn't red tape - it's protection. General-purpose AI platforms, like free chatbots available online, simply aren't designed to handle patient data safely. They may store information on servers in different countries, lack proper encryption, or use your inputs to train their models. Before using any AI tool at work, check your organisation's approved software list and make sure you understand where the data will be processed and stored. Proper contractual safeguards need to be in place with any third-party vendor, covering everything from data encryption to breach notification procedures. If a tool isn't on the approved list, don't use it for patient-related work, no matter how convenient it might seem.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Preparing data safely before it goes anywhere near an AI system is absolutely critical. Anonymisation isn't just about removing someone's name - you need to strip out all direct identifiers like dates of birth, NHS numbers, addresses, and phone numbers. But it goes further than that. You also need to consider indirect identifiers that could combine to reveal someone's identity. For example, age combined with a rare condition and a specific postcode area might be enough to identify someone. Where full anonymisation isn't possible, pseudonymisation can be an alternative - replacing identifiers with codes that can only be linked back by authorised people with the right key. Whatever method you use, document it clearly so others can understand what steps you've taken. This preparation work takes time, but it's what makes the difference between safe and unsafe AI use.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Controlling who can access patient data and monitoring how it's used are fundamental safeguards that apply just as much to AI systems as to traditional records. The principle is simple: people should only have access to the information they genuinely need to do their job. This means regularly reviewing permissions as roles change, people move teams, or responsibilities shift. Every interaction with patient data should be logged to create a clear audit trail - who accessed what, when, and why. Modern systems can also monitor for unusual patterns that might indicate a problem, like someone accessing far more records than usual or looking at information outside their normal area of work. And if something does go wrong, immediate reporting is essential. The sooner a breach is identified and contained, the better the outcome for everyone involved.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring everything together with some core principles to carry forward. The golden rule is simple: never put real patient information into an AI tool. That means no names, no dates of birth, no NHS numbers. Always anonymise your data before you do anything else. And remember, it is not just the obvious details that cause problems. Small pieces of information can combine in ways that make a patient identifiable, even when each piece seems harmless on its own. Before you use any AI tool at work, check that your organisation has actually approved it. Policies exist for good reason, and it is important to follow them. If you are ever unsure whether something is safe to share, the simplest advice is to leave it out entirely. And if you need guidance, please do reach out to your information governance lead. They are there to help you use these tools safely and confidently.
