Welcome to this lesson on using AI with Electronic Health Records. AI tools are becoming a regular part of clinical life, and many of you will already be encountering them in your day-to-day work, whether you realise it or not. But as with any new technology in healthcare, it is not enough to simply know how to use these tools. We also need to understand what can go wrong. Two of the biggest concerns for healthcare staff right now are AI getting things wrong when summarising patient information, and the risk of sensitive data being exposed in ways we might not expect. Throughout this lesson, we will look honestly at both sides: the genuine benefits AI can bring to patient care, and the very real responsibilities that come with it. And do not worry if you are not particularly technical. No specialist knowledge is required here. Just an open mind and a willingness to think carefully about how we use these tools responsibly.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start with one of the most serious risks: patient data exposure. When you use AI tools with patient information, that data often gets processed on external servers that you cannot see or control. Consumer AI applications like ChatGPT are not built with clinical-grade privacy protections, and they may store your input data for training purposes or share it with third parties. Even with approved clinical AI tools, you need to understand where your data is going. Different AI vendors have varying data retention policies, and some may keep patient information longer than necessary. This creates ongoing privacy risks that extend well beyond the immediate clinical interaction. The complexity increases when AI contributes to documentation, because it becomes harder to trace exactly what information came from where, making audit trails more difficult to follow.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Even the most careful clinicians can fall into AI-related traps, and understanding why this happens is crucial for prevention. AI outputs often look polished and confident, which can create a false sense of reliability. When you are working under time pressure, it becomes tempting to skip verification steps and trust what the AI has produced. This over-reliance is particularly dangerous because it reduces your natural clinical scrutiny. Each time you interact with AI systems, you are potentially exposing patient data, and multiple interactions create cumulative risk. The more comfortable you become with AI tools, the more vigilant you need to be about maintaining proper oversight and verification processes.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now, let's talk about what can go wrong when AI summarises a patient's record incorrectly, because the consequences here are genuinely serious. Imagine a clinician glancing at an AI-generated summary and seeing the wrong medication listed, or not seeing a critical allergy flagged at all. That kind of error could lead directly to a patient being harmed. It is not just about drugs and allergies either. If the AI records an incorrect diagnosis, or pulls through information that is months or even years out of date, the clinical picture becomes distorted. Decisions get made based on a version of the patient that no longer reflects reality. This is why AI summarisation in healthcare is not something we can treat casually. The technology can be incredibly useful, but it needs proper oversight. Someone with clinical knowledge must always review what the AI produces, because when it gets things wrong in this setting, patient safety is genuinely on the line.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Your first line of defence is using only approved AI tools and protecting patient data from inappropriate exposure. Every NHS trust and healthcare organisation should have a clear policy about which AI tools are acceptable for clinical use. These approved tools have undergone security and privacy assessments that consumer applications have not. The golden rule is simple: never copy patient details into general AI applications like ChatGPT, because doing so could constitute a serious data breach. If you do suspect that patient data has been inappropriately shared or exposed, report it immediately through your organisation's incident reporting process. And get into the habit of documenting when and how you use AI in clinical tasks. This creates transparency and accountability, protecting both you and your patients.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Beyond using approved tools, you need robust verification processes for every AI output. This means checking every AI-generated summary against the original patient records before making any clinical decisions. Pay particular attention to patient identity markers, because AI systems can sometimes mix up similar patient names or identifiers. Look for temporal inconsistencies, where AI might have pulled old information and presented it as current, or missed recent updates to the patient's condition. Cross-reference critical information like allergies, current medications, and active diagnoses with multiple sources. And always document your verification process in your clinical notes. This shows that you have exercised proper clinical judgement and provides a clear audit trail for your decision-making.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, AI can be incredibly helpful when working with electronic health records, but it is not infallible, and protecting your patients from potential errors is absolutely essential. The first thing to get into the habit of is always checking any summary or suggestion the AI produces. Never simply accept it at face value. Take a moment to verify that the patient details are correct, because even a small mix-up in names, dates, or medication information could have serious consequences. If something looks unusual or unexpected in the AI output, do not ignore it. Flag it straight away and investigate further. It can be tempting, especially when you are busy, to skip the manual review stage, but please do not. That human check is your safety net. And finally, if you do spot an error, report it promptly through the appropriate channels. This helps your organisation learn, improve the system, and ultimately keep every patient safer.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So before we wrap up this lesson, let's bring together the most important things to hold onto. First and foremost, patient data is sensitive and must always be handled with care. Never share it casually or beyond what is necessary. When AI produces a summary or a recommendation, do not simply accept it at face value. Always check it yourself, because mistakes in a clinical setting are not just inconvenient, they can genuinely harm patients. If something does not look right, or if you have concerns about how AI is being used in your workplace, please speak to your supervisor. You are not expected to have all the answers, but you are expected to raise questions when something feels off. And finally, this field is moving quickly, so stay curious and keep building your knowledge over time. The more you understand about these tools, the better placed you will be to use them safely and responsibly.
