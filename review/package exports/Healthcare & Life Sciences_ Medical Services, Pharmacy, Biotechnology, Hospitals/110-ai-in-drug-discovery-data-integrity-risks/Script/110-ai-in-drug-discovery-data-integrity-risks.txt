Welcome to this course on AI in Drug Discovery and Data Integrity Risks. Over the next few lessons, we are going to explore a topic that sits right at the intersection of cutting-edge technology and patient safety. Artificial intelligence is genuinely transforming how we discover and develop new medicines. Processes that once took years can now move significantly faster, which is incredibly exciting. But here is the thing: AI is only as good as the data you feed it. If the underlying data is poor quality or contains errors, those errors do not just sit quietly in a spreadsheet. They get amplified and repeated at enormous scale, potentially influencing decisions that affect real patients. So data integrity, meaning the accuracy, consistency, and reliability of your data, is not just a technical concern. It is a fundamental safety concern. Throughout this course, we will look at where those risks come from and what you can do about them. Let us get started.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So before we can talk about protecting data integrity, we need to understand where the data actually comes from, because the source of your data has a huge influence on the risks it carries. In drug discovery, we're pulling information from all sorts of places. Public databases are incredibly useful, but they're often incomplete or out of date. Lab instruments, as reliable as they are, can malfunction and produce readings that look perfectly normal but are actually wrong. Then there's human error, which is unavoidable when people are manually entering data under pressure. We also frequently encounter legacy data, information that was collected years ago in formats that don't play nicely with modern systems. And finally, third-party sources, whether that's a partner organisation or a commercial data provider, come with their own uncertainties because we simply can't always verify how that data was collected or handled. Each of these sources introduces its own category of risk, and that's what we'll be unpacking throughout this lesson.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
There's an old saying in computing: garbage in, garbage out. And it applies perfectly here. If you feed an AI model poor quality data, whether that's incomplete records, mislabelled compounds, or inconsistent measurements, you're going to get unreliable results. The trouble is, those errors don't just sit in one place. They compound. Each stage of the discovery process builds on the last, so a problem that starts small can quietly grow into something much more serious. Imagine a single corrupted dataset skewing the results of your entire compound screening programme. That's not a hypothetical, it genuinely happens. And what makes this particularly frustrating is that poor data quality often goes unnoticed until very late in the pipeline, by which point you've already spent enormous amounts of time and resource heading in the wrong direction. Ultimately, this isn't just about wasted budgets. At the end of the pipeline are real patients waiting for treatments that work.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the most serious risks in AI-driven drug discovery is population bias in training datasets. Historically, clinical trials have skewed heavily towards Western populations, men, and younger adults. That means the datasets AI systems learn from carry those same gaps and blind spots. When a model is trained on skewed data, it simply cannot predict accurately how a drug will perform in women, older patients, or people from different ethnic backgrounds. Those groups are effectively invisible to the model. And here is why this really matters in practice: if the AI is working from flawed data, it can push a drug candidate further along the development pipeline than it deserves, wasting enormous resources and potentially putting patients at risk. The bias starts early in data collection, and the consequences compound quickly as the AI makes predictions based on incomplete information.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Rare diseases present a particularly challenging scenario for AI in drug discovery. By definition, these conditions affect small patient populations, which means the available data is already limited. But the problem goes beyond just having fewer data points. Any errors or gaps in those small datasets get magnified rather than smoothed out by statistical averaging. You might have incomplete medical histories, missing test results, or very little diversity even within an already tiny patient cohort. This puts AI systems in a difficult position because machine learning algorithms generally perform better with large, comprehensive datasets. When you're working with rare diseases, the AI is trying to learn patterns from insufficient examples, making its predictions inherently less reliable. This creates a real equity issue, where patients with rare conditions may be systematically underserved by AI-driven research approaches.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now let's talk about the foundational practices you need to protect data integrity. Think of data governance as the framework that keeps everything else working properly. Start by validating your training data sources before they go anywhere near your AI pipeline. You need to know where the data came from and whether you can trust it. Implement version control for your datasets, just like developers do for code, so you can track exactly which version was used when. Keep a clear separation between your raw data and anything that has been processed, so the original record is always protected. Document every transformation step along the way, because if something goes wrong, you need to be able to trace it back. These might seem like administrative tasks, but they're actually the foundation of reliable research.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Good data governance sets you up for success, but ongoing monitoring is what keeps you there. Build regular audits into your workflow to spot unusual patterns or anomalies in your outputs before they cause problems downstream. Assign clear ownership of data quality checks to specific team members, rather than assuming everyone will catch issues. Invest in training your research teams so they can recognise common data quality problems early in the process. And if you're just getting started with AI in your organisation, resist the urge to scale up too quickly. Start with something small and manageable, test your results thoroughly, and only expand once you're confident your processes are working reliably. This systematic approach to quality assurance will save you significant time and resources in the long run.

Let us bring everything together with the core principles that will serve you throughout your work with AI in drug discovery. Remember that the quality of your AI output depends entirely on the quality of your input data. Garbage in, garbage out is not just a catchy phrase, it's a fundamental truth. Always validate your data before you use it, and stay vigilant about bias because it corrupts models in ways that aren't always obvious. Build proper audit trails, not as optional extras but as essential safety measures. And ultimately, remember why all of this matters: clean, reliable data protects patient safety and leads to better treatments. If you're just starting your journey with AI, take it step by step. Start small, check your results carefully, and build your confidence from there. These principles will guide you toward responsible and effective AI implementation.
