Slide 1 (Slide 1)

Title: AI in Healthcare Resource Allocation

Subtitle: The current reality of automated decisions

Content:
• Hospitals already use algorithms to assign beds, schedule staff, and distribute equipment
• These systems make consequential decisions, often without direct human review
• Decisions happen in milliseconds rather than hours
• Speed and efficiency can mask issues with fairness or transparency
• Real patients and workers are affected when the logic goes wrong

Slide 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: The Operational Benefits

Subtitle: Why healthcare organisations adopt AI allocation systems

Content:
• Faster decision-making reduces bottlenecks in patient flow
• Consistent application of allocation criteria across all shifts
• Optimisation algorithms can improve resource utilisation rates
• Reduced administrative workload for clinical staff
• 24/7 operation without human fatigue or availability constraints
• Data-driven insights can reveal inefficiencies in current processes

Slide 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Operational Risks of Automation

Subtitle: When systems fail or perform unexpectedly

Content:
• Small errors can scale rapidly across entire organisations
• Over-reliance may erode staff clinical judgement skills
• Edge cases and emergencies often fall outside system design parameters
• Technical failures can create allocation chaos without backup processes
• Staff may not understand how to properly override automated decisions
• Accountability gaps arise when no individual owns the outcome

Slide 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Bias in Training Data

Subtitle: How historical inequalities become algorithmic decisions

Content:
• AI systems trained on historical data inherit past allocation inequalities
• Certain patient groups may be systematically under-prioritised
• Proxy variables like postcode or employment status can introduce hidden discrimination
• Minority populations often received less care in historical datasets
• Algorithms can amplify existing disparities rather than correct them

Slide 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: The Challenge of Defining Fairness

Subtitle: Why algorithmic fairness is technically complex

Content:
• Multiple mathematical definitions of fairness exist and can contradict each other
• Equal treatment may not result in equitable outcomes across different groups
• Fairness requires deliberate testing across demographic categories
• Active monitoring is essential as patient populations and needs change
• Unchecked bias can widen existing health inequalities rather than reduce them

Slide 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Transparency and Accountability

Subtitle: Understanding how decisions are made

Content:
• Black box algorithms make it difficult to understand decision reasoning
• Staff need to know what metrics the system is optimising for
• Lack of explainability complicates error correction and appeals processes
• Unclear responsibility chains emerge when humans and AI share decisions
• Patients and families may struggle to understand why resources were allocated
• Documentation requirements become more complex with automated systems

Slide 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Risk Mitigation Strategies

Subtitle: Practical steps for safer AI implementation

Content:
• Conduct regular audits of AI allocation decisions for bias and accuracy
• Maintain human oversight with clear override protocols
• Flag low-confidence outputs for manual review
• Train staff on when and how to challenge automated decisions
• Log all allocation changes with reasoning for accountability
• Establish clear escalation procedures for edge cases and emergencies

Slide 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Responsible Implementation Framework

Subtitle: Core principles for ethical AI use in healthcare

Content:
• AI supports human decision-making rather than replacing clinical judgement
• Question outputs that contradict clinical experience or seem unusual
• Actively monitor for bias across all patient demographic groups
• Ensure transparency in system objectives and decision criteria
• Maintain clear accountability structures for automated decisions
• Review and update AI governance policies regularly to address emerging risks
