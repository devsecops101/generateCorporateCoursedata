Script 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So far in this course, we have looked at how AI can support drug interaction checks and where it genuinely adds value. But now we need to have an honest conversation about where things can go wrong. Because the truth is, AI does not always get it right, and when it misses something in a clinical setting, the consequences can be serious. Sometimes the system misreads a combination of drugs and flags the wrong risk, or worse, flags nothing at all. Sometimes alerts are generated so frequently that clinicians start to ignore them, which is a real and well-documented problem. There are also situations where the underlying data the AI relies on is simply incomplete or out of date. And because these errors can be subtle, they often slip through without anyone noticing until harm has already occurred.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's look at specific cases where AI systems have failed in drug interaction checking, because these aren't hypothetical risks. There have been documented instances where AI missed fatal drug combinations entirely, leading to serious patient harm. In some cases, the systems provided incomplete alerts that gave healthcare professionals false confidence that everything had been checked properly. Critical gaps in the underlying drug databases meant certain interactions simply weren't flagged. Perhaps most concerning is when healthcare professionals placed too much trust in AI output, assuming the system had caught everything that needed attention. These real-world failures highlight why we can't treat AI as an infallible safety net.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the biggest challenges with AI drug interaction tools is that they're only as good as the data they were trained on. If newer medications aren't included in those datasets, the AI simply won't know how they interact with other drugs. Rare drug combinations are often underrepresented because there aren't enough cases in the training data for the AI to learn from. The knowledge may also be outdated, meaning recently discovered interaction risks haven't been captured yet. This creates a fundamental problem: AI systems are most confident about common drug interactions but may stay completely silent about newer or less documented risks that could be equally dangerous.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
There are entire categories of substances that AI systems often overlook, creating significant blind spots in interaction checking. Herbal remedies and dietary supplements are frequently ignored, despite having real potential for dangerous interactions with prescription medications. When drugs are prescribed for off-label uses, the AI may not recognize those specific applications. Local hospital formularies, with their particular selection of medications, are unlikely to be reflected in generic AI systems. Specialty medications used in specific therapeutic areas may be poorly represented, and even common over-the-counter drugs can be missed in interaction analysis.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Individual patient factors create another layer of complexity that AI systems struggle to handle. Patients with unusual genetic profiles may metabolize drugs differently than the typical patterns AI recognizes. When someone has multiple overlapping medical conditions, the interaction between those conditions and various medications becomes incredibly complex. Age-related changes in metabolism, kidney function, and liver function all affect how drugs behave in the body, but most AI systems don't integrate this physiological data. The result is that patients who don't fit standard profiles may be at higher risk precisely because they fall outside what the AI was designed to recognize.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Even when AI systems do generate alerts, there are practical problems that limit their effectiveness. Polypharmacy cases, where patients are taking five or more medications, can overwhelm the alert logic, making it difficult to prioritize truly dangerous combinations. Many alerts lack dosage context, so the guidance feels too vague to act on confidently. There's also a frustrating inconsistency where systems might flag relatively low-risk combinations while staying silent on genuinely dangerous ones. Without integration with patient-specific factors like kidney or liver function, the alerts can't account for how individual physiology affects drug behavior. All of this contributes to alert fatigue, where clinicians start ignoring warnings because they've lost trust in the system.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Given these limitations, it's essential to have strategies for safely using AI recommendations. Always cross-check AI advice against trusted clinical sources or drug interaction databases. For complex cases, consult with pharmacists who can provide specialized expertise. Make sure you're considering the complete patient medical history, not just what the AI system can see. Think about individual patient factors that the AI may have missed, like age, organ function, or genetic factors. For high-risk drug combinations, use multiple resources rather than relying on a single AI tool. This layered approach helps ensure that important interactions don't slip through the cracks.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's bring everything together with essential guidelines for safe practice. Never rely solely on AI for drug interaction checks - treat it as one tool among many, not a complete safety solution. When you encounter uncertainty or complex cases, always escalate to more experienced colleagues or specialists. If you have concerns about AI-generated advice, document those concerns in the patient record for continuity of care. Verify recommendations against multiple sources, especially for unfamiliar drug combinations. Most importantly, maintain your clinical judgment as the primary safety check. AI can support your decision-making, but it cannot replace the comprehensive assessment that only an experienced healthcare professional can provide.
