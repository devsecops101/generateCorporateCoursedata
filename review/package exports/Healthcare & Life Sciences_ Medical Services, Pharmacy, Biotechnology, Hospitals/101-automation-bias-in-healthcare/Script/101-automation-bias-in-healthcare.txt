Welcome to this course on automation bias in healthcare. As artificial intelligence becomes more common in clinical settings, it is being used to help with everything from reading scans to flagging deteriorating patients. And on the whole, that is a positive development. But there is a risk that comes with it, one that does not always get the attention it deserves. Sometimes, clinicians accept what an AI system tells them without stopping to apply their own professional judgement. This tendency has a name: automation bias. And when it goes unchecked, the consequences can be serious. We are talking about missed diagnoses, treatments that are delayed, and in the worst cases, real harm to patients. This course is designed to help you understand where that risk comes from and what you can do about it. Because the first step toward using AI safely in practice is simply knowing that this problem exists.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
What exactly do we mean by automation bias? At its core, it's the tendency to trust what an AI system tells us, sometimes more than we trust our own professional judgement. And here's where it gets interesting: it doesn't just mean blindly following bad advice. It also shows up in the smaller moments, like skipping a check you'd normally do simply because the AI has already flagged something, or more often, because it hasn't. In busy, high-pressure clinical environments, this kind of shortcut can feel completely natural. You're stretched, the system seems reliable, and so you lean on it. The tricky part is that automation bias is genuinely subtle. It doesn't feel like a mistake when it's happening. And importantly, this isn't about inexperience. Research consistently shows that even highly experienced clinicians are affected. So as we work through this course, I'd encourage you to keep an open mind about where this might show up in your own practice.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how does automation bias actually show up in day-to-day clinical work? Often it is quite subtle. It might be that you glance at an AI-generated diagnosis and simply move on, without checking whether it matches what you are actually seeing in front of you. Or perhaps you skip a second review because the system has already given you an answer, so it feels unnecessary. Sometimes it shows up as a reluctance to override an alert, almost a feeling of "well, the system flagged it, so who am I to argue?" It can also appear in your documentation, where you stop recording your own clinical reasoning because the AI recommendation is already sitting there on screen. And there is a team dynamic to watch for as well. When an AI output is visible in the room, conversations tend to get shorter, and people stop questioning as much. These are the early warning signs, and learning to spot them is the first step towards safer practice.

Script 5 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How do we actually spot automation bias happening in our own practice? It can be surprisingly subtle. One of the most common signs is when you find yourself accepting an alert or a recommendation without really questioning it, almost on autopilot. Or perhaps you notice you have skipped your own clinical assessment because the system has already flagged something. Another sign is feeling reassured very quickly, simply because the technology has not raised an alarm, even when something feels off. We also tend to defer more to automated systems when we are feeling uncertain or lacking in confidence, which is completely understandable but worth being aware of. And perhaps most importantly, watch out for moments when you dismiss or overlook what the patient in front of you is telling you, because it does not match what the system is showing. Recognising these patterns in yourself is genuinely the first step towards making safer, more balanced clinical decisions.

Script 7 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How do we actually push back against automation bias in our day-to-day practice? It starts with something simple: just pause before you accept what the AI is telling you. Give yourself a moment to think rather than clicking through on autopilot. And pay particular attention when results come back looking completely normal, especially if that surprises you, because that is often where errors hide. It also helps to bring colleagues into your decision-making process. A second pair of eyes can catch what you might have missed, and it reduces the pressure to rely solely on the machine. Beyond that, do not underestimate your own clinical instincts. If something feels off, it probably warrants a closer look, regardless of what the algorithm says. And finally, get into the habit of writing down your own reasoning. Documenting your thought process keeps you actively engaged and creates a clear record that is independent of the AI output.

Script 8 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
AI tools are genuinely useful, but they are there to support your thinking, not replace it. Always verify what they suggest. Be especially mindful when you are tired or under pressure, because that is precisely when automation bias tends to creep in. And remember, just because an output looks polished and confident does not mean it is correct. One practical habit worth building is a deliberate pause before you act on any AI recommendation. Give yourself a moment to ask, "Does this actually make sense to me?" Ultimately, your clinical judgement is the final safeguard, and nothing about AI changes that. To get started, pick just one workflow this week and practise applying active oversight there. Small steps build strong habits, and that is really where lasting change begins.
