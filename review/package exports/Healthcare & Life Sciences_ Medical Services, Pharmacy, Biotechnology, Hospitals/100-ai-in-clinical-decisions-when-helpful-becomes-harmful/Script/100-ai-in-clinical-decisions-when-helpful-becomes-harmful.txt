Over the past few years, AI tools have moved quickly into healthcare settings. They are faster, cheaper, and more accessible than ever before, and many clinicians are now routinely using them to help with diagnosis and treatment decisions. On the surface, that sounds like progress, and in many ways it is. But there is a problem worth taking seriously. AI can produce outputs that look confident and convincing, yet contain errors that are genuinely difficult to spot. The system does not hesitate, it does not flag uncertainty the way a cautious colleague might, and that can make it easy to trust something you probably should not. So this course is about developing a critical eye. Understanding where AI tends to go wrong, and why, is no longer just useful background knowledge for clinicians. It is becoming an essential part of safe practice. Let us start by looking at exactly where that fine line sits.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start by thinking about how AI actually processes medical information, because this is where things get interesting. AI systems are extraordinarily good at spotting patterns across huge datasets. They can look at thousands of scans or test results and identify recurring features that might signal a particular condition. But here's the important thing to understand: spotting a pattern is not the same as understanding what's causing it. AI has no real grasp of a patient's full history, their lifestyle, their previous illnesses, or the subtle clinical details that an experienced clinician picks up during a consultation. It can struggle significantly with rare conditions or complex cases that simply don't fit the patterns it was trained on. And perhaps most critically, it can confuse correlation with an actual diagnosis. Just because two things appear together in data doesn't mean one is causing the other. That distinction matters enormously in clinical practice, and AI doesn't always make it reliably.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the most dangerous things about AI in clinical settings is how convincing it sounds, even when it is completely wrong. These systems don't have an internal warning light that flickers when they're uncertain. They produce text based on patterns in data, not because they genuinely understand a patient's condition or the nuances of disease. So a confident, well-structured response and a completely fabricated one can look identical on screen. As clinicians, we're trained to trust authoritative sources, and that instinct can work against us here. When something reads like a textbook, we naturally lower our guard. But the fluency of the language tells you nothing about the accuracy of the content. Just because an AI states something with apparent certainty doesn't mean the underlying evidence supports it. Recognising this gap between how confident the output sounds and how reliable it actually is, is absolutely fundamental to using these tools safely.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
There's an old saying in computing: garbage in, garbage out. And it applies perfectly here. AI systems learn from historical data, and if that data is flawed or unrepresentative, the model will simply learn and repeat those flaws. The problem is that many medical datasets have been built over decades during which certain groups, particularly ethnic minorities, women, and older patients, were consistently underrepresented in research and clinical trials. So when an AI is trained on that data, it doesn't just inherit those gaps, it amplifies them. A diagnostic tool trained mostly on data from one demographic may perform brilliantly for that group and quite poorly for everyone else. And here's the really concerning part: the system doesn't know it's doing this. It just produces an output that looks confident and authoritative. That's why understanding where the training data comes from, and who it represents, is absolutely fundamental before trusting any AI tool with clinical decisions.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Most clinical AI tools are trained on historical data, and that means any biases buried in that data get carried forward into the outputs, often without anyone noticing. What makes this particularly dangerous is that AI doesn't hesitate. It can be completely wrong and still sound utterly confident. It also has no way of knowing what's missing. If a patient history is incomplete, the AI won't flag that gap, it will just work with what it's been given. Rare conditions are another blind spot, because they're underrepresented in training data, so the AI is far less likely to surface them as possibilities. And perhaps most importantly, AI is doing pattern-matching across large populations. It isn't reasoning about the specific person sitting in front of you. It has no sense of urgency, no awareness of context, and absolutely no equivalent of a clinician's gut instinct. These aren't minor limitations. They matter enormously in practice.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
We've seen what AI can do, but let's be honest about where it falls short. Even the most sophisticated AI system cannot do what an experienced clinician does naturally, which is hold the full picture of a patient in mind. It doesn't pick up on the subtle things, the slight hesitation in someone's voice, the way they carry themselves, or the years of lived experience they bring into the consultation room. It also struggles when a patient presents in an unusual or atypical way, because if that pattern wasn't well represented in the training data, the AI simply won't handle it well. And it has no awareness of your local protocols, what resources are actually available, or what matters most to that individual patient. This is the really important point: AI is doing pattern-matching. It's finding similarities in data. That is genuinely useful, but it is not the same thing as clinical judgement, and we should never confuse the two.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's talk about some warning signs you really need to keep in mind when you're using AI tools in clinical settings. First, be cautious if the AI sounds extremely confident, because that certainty isn't always earned. If there's no source cited for the information it's giving you, that's a problem straight away. You have no way of knowing where that output has come from. AI systems can also struggle with rare or unusual conditions, so if something doesn't quite fit the picture, don't assume the AI has considered every possibility. And importantly, if the output contradicts what you know from your training and clinical experience, please don't just accept it. Your expertise matters enormously here. Perhaps the biggest red flag of all is when AI output simply isn't questioned at all, either by you or your colleagues. These tools should always be scrutinised, not followed blindly. Recognising these warning signs is genuinely the first step towards using AI safely and responsibly.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Bringing everything together with a few core principles to carry forward. Think of AI as a tool that supports your thinking, not something that makes decisions for you. Whatever it suggests, you verify it. You check it against your clinical knowledge, your patient in front of you, and your professional judgement. Patient safety is always the priority, full stop. If something feels off, or you are unsure about an AI output, escalate. Talk to a colleague, a senior, whoever is appropriate. Do not let uncertainty sit in silence. And this is perhaps the most important point of all: the clinical responsibility remains with you. Not the algorithm, not the software, not the organisation that deployed it. You. That means staying alert, asking questions, and speaking up if you have concerns about how AI is being used in your workplace. These principles are straightforward, but keeping them front of mind will genuinely help you use AI more safely and more confidently.
