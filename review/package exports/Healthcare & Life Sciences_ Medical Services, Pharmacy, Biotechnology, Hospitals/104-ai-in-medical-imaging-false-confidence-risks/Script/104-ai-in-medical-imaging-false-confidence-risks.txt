Welcome to this course on AI in medical imaging, and specifically the risks that come with false confidence. Artificial intelligence has genuinely transformed the way we analyse medical scans. It can process images at remarkable speed, flagging potential issues far faster than a human radiologist working alone. That sounds impressive, and in many ways it is. But here is the important part: speed does not equal accuracy. AI systems still make errors, and those errors can mean missed diagnoses, delayed treatment, and real harm to patients. What makes this particularly tricky is that AI often presents its findings with a kind of quiet authority, and that can subtly nudge clinicians into trusting the output without questioning it. Throughout this course, we are going to explore why that misplaced confidence is so dangerous, and why keeping a human expert firmly in the loop is not optional. It is absolutely essential.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, what do we actually mean by false confidence risks? At its core, this is about what happens when an AI system appears certain, but is actually wrong. Many AI tools display a confidence score, something like "95% probability of normal findings," but that number doesn't always reflect how reliable the result truly is in a real clinical setting. The danger comes when a clinician sees that high score and stops there, without applying their own professional judgement. Imagine an AI flagging a scan as clear when there's actually a tumour present. If that result is accepted without a second look, the consequences for the patient can be severe. And it works both ways. AI can miss things it should catch, but it can also flag things that aren't there, leading to unnecessary interventions.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the most insidious aspects of false confidence is how gradually it develops. It doesn't happen overnight. A clinician starts seeing high confidence scores and begins to rely on them more heavily. Time pressure in clinical settings makes this worse, as there's natural pressure to work quickly and efficiently. When an AI system confidently flags something as normal, it's tempting to move on without double-checking. This creates a dangerous form of anchoring bias, where the AI output becomes the starting point for all subsequent thinking. The problem is particularly acute with subtle findings, because those are exactly the cases where we most need careful human analysis, yet they're also the ones where AI might appear most confident while being completely wrong.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Understanding why AI systems have blind spots is crucial for safe practice. These systems learn from training datasets, but those datasets are never complete representations of all possible medical conditions. Rare diseases might have only a handful of examples in the training data, making it nearly impossible for the AI to recognize them reliably. Poor image quality from patient movement or equipment issues can completely throw off an algorithm that was trained on perfect laboratory conditions. Similarly, certain patient populations might be underrepresented in the training data, creating systematic biases. When a condition presents in an unusual way, the pattern-matching approach that AI uses can fail spectacularly, yet the system may still output a confident-looking result.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's be very clear about what confidence scores actually mean, because this is where a lot of confusion happens. When an AI system shows you a 95% confidence rating, it's not saying there's a 95% chance the finding is correct. It's saying that based on the patterns it learned during training, it's very certain about its output. These are fundamentally different things. The score reflects how the model behaved on its training data, not how accurate it will be in your specific clinical environment with your patients. This distinction becomes critical with subtle or early-stage lesions, which are often the most important to catch. These are precisely the cases where AI might display false certainty, appearing confident while missing something crucial.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
There are specific scenarios where AI systems consistently struggle, and recognizing these can help you stay alert to potential problems. Rare conditions are the most obvious example, simply because the AI hasn't seen enough cases to learn reliable patterns. Motion artifacts, poor contrast, or suboptimal imaging parameters can completely confuse algorithms that were trained on high-quality images. When diseases present atypically, or when multiple conditions overlap in complex ways, the pattern-recognition approach that AI relies on starts to break down. These edge cases, situations that fall outside the parameters of the training data, are where human expertise becomes absolutely irreplaceable. The challenge is that AI systems don't necessarily signal when they're operating in these problematic areas.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, knowing the risks is one thing, but what can you actually do about them in day-to-day practice? The most important habit to build is treating AI output as a starting point, not a final answer. Always run your own clinical assessment alongside whatever the system flags, and crucially, check the areas it did not flag just as carefully as the ones it did. Structured checklists are really useful here because they prevent you from unconsciously deferring to the AI's confidence score rather than your own judgement. If something doesn't add up, report it. Those discrepancies help improve systems over time and protect your patients right now. And whenever the AI output conflicts with what you're seeing clinically, bring in a senior colleague.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Perhaps the most important long-term consideration is preventing your own diagnostic skills from eroding due to over-reliance on AI. This happens more subtly than you might expect. Regular training and practice interpreting images without AI assistance helps maintain your independent abilities. Stay informed about AI limitations and system updates, because these tools are evolving rapidly. Most importantly, never stay silent when something feels wrong or when you're uncertain about an AI output. Raise concerns with colleagues, flag issues to supervisors, and remember that your clinical judgement is irreplaceable. AI should enhance your diagnostic capabilities, not replace them. The goal is to use these powerful tools safely and effectively, always keeping patient welfare as the top priority.
