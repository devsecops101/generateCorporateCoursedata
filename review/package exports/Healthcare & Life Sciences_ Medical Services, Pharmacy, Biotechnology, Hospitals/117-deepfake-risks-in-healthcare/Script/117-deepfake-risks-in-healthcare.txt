Welcome to this course on deepfake risks in healthcare. You may have heard the term "deepfake" before, but what does it actually mean in a healthcare context? Put simply, deepfakes use artificial intelligence to generate convincing fake audio, video, or images that make it look or sound as though a real person said or did something they never actually did. This technology is becoming a serious threat in healthcare settings. Criminals are using it to impersonate doctors, nurses, and administrators, tricking patients and staff into following false instructions or accepting fraudulent prescriptions. The consequences can be genuinely life-threatening. Both healthcare workers and patients are increasingly targeted by these attacks. Throughout this course, we'll explore how these threats work, where they appear, and most importantly, what you can do to protect yourself and those in your care.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Deepfake attacks in healthcare take several common forms, and understanding these scenarios helps you stay alert. You might receive a video message that appears to show your doctor providing updated medication instructions - looking entirely authentic. Or you could get a phone call from someone who sounds exactly like a consultant you know well, asking you to modify a treatment plan. Fraudsters also create fake audio messages that seem to come from pharmacy staff, requesting prescription changes. Sometimes they impersonate patients themselves, asking for treatment modifications. Even administrative directives can be faked, affecting entire care protocols. These attacks work because healthcare relies on trust relationships between team members. Criminals exploit this trust, knowing that healthcare workers are trained to respond quickly to clinical instructions, especially in urgent situations.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When viewing any video communication, train your eye to spot visual inconsistencies that could indicate a deepfake. Watch the person's face carefully - if facial movements look unnatural or if the lips don't perfectly sync with the words being spoken, that's a warning sign. Pay attention to lighting as well. Genuine video calls typically have consistent lighting, while deepfakes often show odd shadows or an unusual glow around the face. Look for unnatural blinking patterns or strange eye movements, which current deepfake technology often struggles to replicate convincingly. The background can also provide clues - watch for distortions or inconsistencies that don't match what you'd expect from that person's usual location. These technical imperfections are often subtle, but developing an eye for them can help you identify fraudulent content.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Audio deepfakes have their own telltale signs that you can learn to recognize. Listen carefully to the voice quality - deepfake voices often sound slightly robotic or unnaturally flat, lacking the full emotional range of genuine human speech. Pay attention to speech patterns too. Unnatural pauses, odd rhythm, or inconsistent audio quality throughout the conversation can all be indicators. Beyond the technical aspects, consider the content. A real person would typically include personal conversational details or references that only they would know. If the caller seems to avoid direct questions about shared experiences or personal details, that's suspicious. Also notice if they seem to dodge straightforward questions about their identity or recent interactions you've had. The background audio should also match where the person claims to be calling from.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The content of suspicious communications often reveals itself through certain patterns. Be particularly cautious of unexpected changes to patient medication dosages or treatment plans, especially when they arrive through unofficial channels like personal email or messaging apps. Deepfake attacks frequently create artificial urgency, pressuring you to act immediately and bypass your normal verification procedures. This urgency is deliberate - it's designed to prevent you from pausing to think critically. Watch for instructions that seem to contradict established medical protocols or your organization's standard procedures. Any request to share patient data outside of your approved systems should raise immediate red flags. Most importantly, if you cannot trace a clinical instruction back to an official, documented source within your organization's systems, treat it with serious suspicion and always verify before acting.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The most effective defense against deepfakes is systematic verification through trusted channels. Never rely solely on the apparent identity of the caller or message sender. Instead, always verify through a separate communication method - if someone calls you, hang up and call them back using an official number from your organization's records. When confirming identity, ask for personal details that only the real person would know about your recent interactions or shared experiences. Cross-check any clinical instructions against your documented protocols and official medical records. Most importantly, resist pressure to act immediately, no matter how urgent the request seems. Genuine medical emergencies have established protocols that include proper verification steps. Remember, taking time to verify is not a sign of inefficiency - it's a sign of professional responsibility and patient safety awareness.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
If you encounter anything that seems suspicious, follow these clear steps immediately. First and most importantly, stop - do not act on whatever instruction or request you've received, no matter how legitimate it appeared. Next, notify your line manager without delay, as they need to be aware of potential security threats immediately. While the details are still fresh in your mind, document exactly what you observed - the time, platform, specific content, and any unusual characteristics you noticed. This documentation could be crucial for investigation and preventing future attacks. Contact your IT security team as soon as possible, as they're trained to handle these situations and can take immediate protective measures. Finally, follow your organization's specific reporting procedures, as different healthcare facilities have varying protocols for handling security incidents.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's consolidate the key habits that will protect you and your patients from deepfake threats. The most critical practice is to verify every clinical instruction through official channels before acting, regardless of how authentic the source appears. Trust your professional instincts - if something feels unusual or off, investigate further rather than dismissing the feeling. Remember that modern deepfake technology can convincingly replicate the appearance and voice of real clinicians you know and trust. Understand that creating artificial urgency is a common tactic used to prevent careful verification, so always resist pressure to bypass standard procedures. When you encounter suspicious content, report it immediately to protect not just yourself, but your colleagues and patients. Most importantly, make verification a routine part of your professional practice - it's not about doubting your colleagues, it's about maintaining the highest standards of patient safety in an evolving threat landscape.
