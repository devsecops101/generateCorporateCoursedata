Welcome, and thank you for joining this course on AI chatbots for patient interaction. Over the past few years, AI chatbots have moved from something quite futuristic into something genuinely everyday. Patients are already using them, whether that is to check symptoms, ask about medications, or simply try to make sense of a diagnosis they have just received. And because this is happening right now, in the real world, it is really important that healthcare professionals understand the risks involved. Those risks are real, and in some cases they are serious, so this course is designed to walk you through the key ones in a straightforward, practical way. You do not need any technical background or prior knowledge of AI to follow along. Everything will be explained clearly as we go. By the end, you will have a much clearer picture of where these tools can cause harm and what that means for the patients in your care. So let us get started.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now, before we get too excited about the possibilities, we need to have an honest conversation about where things can go wrong. When a patient describes their symptoms to an AI chatbot, the system might misjudge how serious the situation actually is. Unlike a clinician, it cannot probe deeper, ask a follow-up question, or pick up on something that does not quite add up. And here is the real concern: patients often trust what they read on screen and may not bother seeking further help if the chatbot gives them a reassuring but incorrect response. This risk is even greater for elderly patients or those who struggle to evaluate health information critically. They are less likely to question what the chatbot tells them. And if the system misses just one red flag, one symptom that should have triggered an urgent referral, the consequences can be genuinely serious. So these are not small technical glitches. They are patient safety issues, and we need to treat them as such.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now, this is where things get really serious, and I want you to pay close attention here. When AI chatbots are used for triage, there is a genuine risk that they miss critical warning signs that a trained clinician would catch immediately. For example, a patient describing chest pain might be told it sounds like anxiety, when in reality it could be a heart attack. Stroke symptoms might be dismissed as stress. Sepsis, which is a life-threatening condition, could go unrecognised, causing dangerous delays in treatment. And here is the core problem: most chatbots are not designed to escalate a conversation to a real clinician when something sounds alarming. They follow pre-set algorithms, and algorithms simply do not have the clinical judgement that comes from years of medical training and human intuition. So whilst chatbots can be genuinely useful, we must be very clear about where their limitations lie, particularly when patient safety is at stake.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Beyond specific missed diagnoses, we need to understand the broader systemic problems with AI triage. These systems often assign the wrong priority level to patient concerns, meaning someone who needs immediate attention might be told to wait days for an appointment. The algorithms struggle with conditions that depend heavily on context or subtle presentations that don't fit standard patterns. Most concerning is that many systems operate without meaningful human oversight, so there's nobody to catch these mistakes before they impact patient care. When patients follow harmful recommendations because they trust the technology, the consequences can be severe. The fundamental issue is that these systems are often designed for efficiency rather than safety, without adequate fallback mechanisms when things go wrong.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now, let's talk about something really important: the ways AI chatbots can actually damage the relationship between patients and their healthcare providers. Trust is fragile, and there are several ways a chatbot can chip away at it. If a chatbot provides inaccurate medical information, even once, a patient may never fully trust it again. Similarly, if someone shares a concern and feels like the system isn't really listening or responding to their specific situation, they'll quickly disengage. There are also genuine worries around data privacy. Patients want to know their personal health information is safe, and any doubt about that can be deeply unsettling. And finally, if the tone feels cold or robotic, patients may simply feel alienated. When these factors combine, the damage extends beyond the chatbot itself to the entire healthcare organisation.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the most significant risks we face with AI chatbots in healthcare is what happens when patients trust them too much. It might sound like a good problem to have, but it genuinely isn't. If someone receives a reassuring response from a chatbot, they may decide they don't need to see a doctor, and in some cases, that delay could be serious. We also need to think carefully about vulnerable patients, those who may be anxious, isolated, or less health-literate, because they are particularly susceptible to over-relying on automated responses. The thing is, a chatbot simply cannot pick up on tone of voice, body language, or the kind of subtle cues a clinician would notice immediately. That's why building clear boundaries into the chatbot's design, right from the start, is so important. The system needs to actively direct patients to human care when appropriate, rather than leaving that decision entirely to them.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When an AI chatbot makes a mistake, how you respond matters enormously. The first thing to do is acknowledge it straight away, without making excuses or blaming the technology. Then apologise clearly, in plain, simple language the patient can actually understand. After that, explain what happened. Patients deserve to know why things went wrong, not just that they did. From there, tell them what your organisation is doing to make sure it does not happen again. That kind of transparency goes a long way towards rebuilding confidence. You should also make it easy for the patient to speak with a real clinician if they want to, because sometimes a human conversation is exactly what is needed to restore trust. Finally, make sure the incident is properly documented and reported through your usual procedures. Getting this process right is not just about one patient. It protects everyone who interacts with your service going forward.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring this all together with some practical guidelines for implementation. AI chatbots can genuinely help with patient interaction, but they are a support tool, not a replacement for clinical judgement. That distinction really matters, especially when it comes to triage or medical advice, where getting things wrong can have serious consequences. Whatever a chatbot tells you or your patients, always verify it before acting on it. And remember that patients are placing real trust in your organisation, so being open and honest about when AI is involved is absolutely essential. The golden rule here is to keep a human in the loop at every stage. Now, as a practical next step, I'd encourage you to go and look at your organisation's current policy on AI tools in patient-facing services. If one doesn't exist yet, that's important information in itself. Remember, patient safety must always come before operational efficiency when implementing these technologies.
