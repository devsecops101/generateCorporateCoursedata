Script 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Welcome to this lesson on AI triage systems and the very real risks that come with getting them wrong. Triage is fundamentally about sorting patients by urgency, making sure the people who need care most urgently are seen first. AI systems are increasingly being used to help make those decisions, and in many ways they can be genuinely useful. But here's the problem: sometimes the algorithm gets it wrong. And when it does, the consequences aren't just a minor inconvenience. We're talking about real patients whose conditions are serious being pushed down the queue, delayed, or missed altogether. That can mean real harm, and in the worst situations, it can be life-threatening. Understanding where these risks lie is absolutely essential if we want to prevent them from causing harm in the first place.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's get clear on what we actually mean by AI triage misclassification. In simple terms, it's when an AI system gets the priority order wrong. It might assess a patient as low urgency when they're actually in a serious condition, or flag someone as high priority when they could safely wait. Both scenarios cause real problems. When a genuinely critical case gets overlooked, treatment is delayed, and that delay can directly harm the patient. The issue that makes this particularly tricky is that these errors aren't always easy to spot. Unlike a system that crashes, a misclassification can look perfectly reasonable on the surface. The AI has made a decision, appears confident, and staff may not immediately question it.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So what actually causes an AI triage system to get it wrong? There are several technical factors worth understanding. If the training data doesn't reflect the full range of patients, the system will struggle with anyone outside that profile. Poor quality inputs, whether that's a muffled phone call or unclear text, can cause the system to misread something critical. Rare but serious conditions are particularly risky because they appear so infrequently in training data that the system may not recognise them. Some systems place too much weight on a single measurement, like heart rate, rather than looking at the whole clinical picture. And historical bias gets baked right in when the data the system learned from already contained human errors or inequalities.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Certain patient groups face higher risks of misclassification due to predictable patterns in how AI systems fail. Older adults and non-native speakers are often underrepresented in training datasets, making accurate assessment less reliable. Patients whose conditions present atypically can completely fool systems trained on standard presentations. Those with rare diseases suffer because these conditions simply don't appear enough in training data for reliable recognition. Complex cases requiring contextual assessment, like considering a patient's living situation or recent travel, often exceed what AI systems can handle. And patients who describe symptoms in everyday language rather than medical terminology may not be properly understood by the algorithm.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When an AI triage system gets it wrong and underestimates urgency, the consequences can be devastating. Conditions like sepsis and stroke are time-critical - we're talking about minutes, not hours, before permanent damage occurs. If a patient is misclassified as low priority, they join the wrong queue and wait while their condition quietly deteriorates. That delay in treatment can change everything, from whether someone recovers fully to whether they survive at all. It's not just the patient who's affected either. Families trying to make decisions and clinicians trying to provide care are all working from inaccurate information. They can't act appropriately if the picture they've been given is wrong.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The impact of misclassification extends well beyond individual patient harm. When families watch a loved one deteriorate because something was missed, trust in the healthcare system can collapse quickly. This creates legal liability for preventable outcomes that could have been avoided with proper triage. Healthcare staff become demoralised when they realise urgent cases have been overlooked, affecting the entire team's confidence in their tools. Resources get allocated inefficiently when the wrong patients receive priority, creating bottlenecks and delays throughout the system. And ultimately, quality metrics suffer and institutional reputation takes a hit when preventable errors come to light.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How do we actually reduce the risk of misclassification in practice? There are concrete steps organisations can take. AI outputs should be regularly audited against real patient outcomes to spot patterns where the system fails. Clinicians must always have the ability to override the AI, and this should never feel like a last resort. Training data needs frequent review because patient populations and conditions change over time. When the AI signals low confidence, there should be clear pathways to escalate to human review. Staff need training to think critically about AI recommendations rather than accepting them at face value. And when errors occur, they should feed back into the system for continuous improvement.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's bring everything together with core lessons for your practice. Remember that AI triage systems are not infallible - they can and do miss urgent cases, so never assume the system has caught everything. Always apply your own clinical judgement rather than simply accepting what the system tells you. Human oversight isn't optional here, it's absolutely essential to safe practice. When something goes wrong or you suspect it might have, report it promptly. Those reports help organisations identify patterns and improve systems over time. And advocate for quality training data that reflects the full diversity of patients you serve. Keep these points in mind and you'll be far better placed to use AI triage tools safely and responsibly.
