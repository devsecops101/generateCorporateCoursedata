Script 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When an AI system is operating in the field, it is making decisions that have real consequences for real people. And here is the critical question this course is built around: who actually owns those decisions? Because ownership does not assign itself. If nobody has clearly defined who is responsible, then in practice, nobody is, and that is when things go wrong. What we will explore throughout this course is how responsibility is shared across different roles, whether you are an operator working directly with the system, an engineer who helped build or configure it, or a leader who approved its deployment. Everyone carries a portion of that accountability. The important thing to understand right now, right at the start, is that this is not something you sort out after a problem occurs. Defining who owns what, before the system goes live, saves an enormous amount of confusion, conflict, and cost down the line. That is where we begin.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's start by getting clear on what we actually mean by accountability, because it's a word that gets used a lot but isn't always well understood. Accountability isn't just about following a process correctly. It's about owning the outcome of a decision, whatever that outcome turns out to be. Now, when AI is involved, some people assume the machine carries that responsibility. It doesn't. The system makes a suggestion, but a human being decides what to do with it. And accountability extends far beyond whoever happens to press the final button or flip the switch. The person who acted was working within systems, policies, and constraints that other people created. Getting that clarity sorted in advance is what stops everyone pointing fingers at each other when something goes wrong.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When we talk about AI in the field, there are really three distinct groups of people involved, and each one plays a very different role. You have the operators, the people on the ground who are actually configuring and deploying these systems within their specific environment. Then you have the engineers, who build and maintain the tools in the first place. And above all of that, you have leadership, who set the boundaries and define what is and is not acceptable. Now, here is the important bit. These roles are not interchangeable, and the accountability that comes with each one is not shared equally across the group. That might sound a little uncomfortable, but it is actually a good thing. When everyone knows exactly what they are responsible for, you close the gaps. Because those gaps, the spaces where nobody feels quite sure whose job it is, that is precisely where things go wrong. Clear roles mean clear ownership, and that is what we are building towards in this lesson.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So when we talk about accountability in AI systems, it is tempting to point at one person and say, 'that's the one responsible.' But in practice, it simply does not work that way. The operator in the field is making real-time decisions, but they are working within a system that engineers designed and that leaders approved. If something goes wrong, you cannot just blame the person who pressed the button, because the rules, the tools, and the training all played a part. And here is the thing: those roles are not neatly separated. An engineer might also be a team leader. A field operator might also be contributing feedback that shapes future system design. The boundaries blur constantly. What this means is that accountability is genuinely shared across the whole organisation. Everyone has a stake in how these decisions play out, and everyone carries a portion of the responsibility when they do not go as planned.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So when we talk about shared accountability, what does that actually look like day to day? Well, it starts with operators. They are the ones working closest to the AI system, following the right procedures and speaking up straight away if something looks off. Then you have engineers, who keep the systems running properly and are honest about what those systems cannot do. And leadership? Their job is to set the rules of the game, deciding where AI can and cannot be used. The important point here is that no single person carries all of this on their own. Accountability is spread across the whole team. But that only works if everyone understands where their responsibility ends and someone else's begins. When those handoffs are clear, you avoid the dangerous situation where everyone assumes somebody else is watching. Shared accountability is not about spreading blame. It is about making sure nothing falls through the cracks.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When something does go wrong with an AI system, the natural human reaction is to look for someone to blame. But that approach misses the point entirely. In most cases, multiple people contributed to the problem, even if they did not realize it at the time. The operator might have followed procedure, but maybe the procedure was flawed. The engineer might have built the system correctly, but maybe the requirements were unclear. The leader might have approved the deployment, but maybe they were not told about certain limitations. Instead of pointing fingers, the focus should be on understanding how the whole system failed and what each role can do differently next time. Good documentation helps here because it lets you trace the decision path and see where things started to go off track. When you approach problems this way, you learn from them instead of just finding someone to punish.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Knowing when and how to escalate concerns about AI decisions is a critical skill that every role needs to develop. If you are an operator and the AI is giving you recommendations that feel wrong, do not just override it quietly—flag it up the chain. If you are an engineer and you know the system has limitations that could affect field decisions, make sure those limitations are clearly communicated. If you are in leadership and AI use is pushing beyond what your organization is prepared to handle, that is when you step in and reset the boundaries. The key is having clear escalation paths so people know exactly who to contact and when. There should never be a situation where someone stays quiet about a concern because they do not know how to raise it or they are worried about looking foolish. Speaking up when AI outputs are questionable protects everyone involved.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So as we close out this lesson, let me leave you with the things that really matter. No matter how capable AI becomes, you remain responsible for every decision made in the field. That never changes. Get into the habit of questioning what AI tells you rather than simply accepting it at face value. And if something feels off or you are not sure, escalate it. There is no shame in asking for a second opinion. When AI is involved in a decision, write it down. Documentation protects you, your team, and the people you serve. Finally, remember that the culture around AI use starts at the top. When leaders model careful, thoughtful behaviour, everyone else follows. These principles are not complicated, but they do require consistency. Carry them with you every time you step into the field, and you will be in a much stronger position to use AI responsibly and effectively.
