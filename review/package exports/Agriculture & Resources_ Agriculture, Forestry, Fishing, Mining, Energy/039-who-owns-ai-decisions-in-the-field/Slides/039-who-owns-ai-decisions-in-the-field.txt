Slide 1 (Slide 1)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Who Owns AI Decisions?

Subtitle: Accountability starts before deployment begins.

Content:
• AI systems in the field make decisions that affect real people and real outcomes
• Someone must own those decisions
• Accountability cannot be left to chance or assumed by default
• Operators, engineers, and leadership each carry a share of responsibility
• Defining ownership early prevents costly confusion later

Slide 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: What Accountability Means

Subtitle: Owning outcomes, not just processes.

Content:
• Accountability means owning the outcome of a decision, not just following procedures
• In AI-assisted work, humans remain responsible for acting on system suggestions
• The machine doesn't carry responsibility—people do
• Ownership extends beyond the person who pressed the button
• Clear accountability prevents blame-shifting when problems occur

Slide 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Three Key Roles

Subtitle: Operators, engineers, and leadership each have distinct responsibilities.

Content:
• Operators configure and deploy AI systems within their specific context
• Engineers build and maintain the underlying tools and systems
• Leadership sets policies and boundaries for acceptable AI use
• Each role carries distinct accountability, not shared blame
• Clear role definition prevents gaps where no one feels responsible

Slide 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: How Roles Connect

Subtitle: Why accountability spans multiple people.

Content:
• Operators make decisions using tools that engineers designed
• Engineers build systems within policies that leadership approved
• Leaders depend on feedback from operators and engineers
• Role boundaries blur in practice—people wear multiple hats
• When something goes wrong, multiple roles contributed to the outcome

Slide 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Daily Accountability Tasks

Subtitle: What each role does to maintain responsibility.

Content:
• Operators follow procedures and flag anomalies in real time
• Engineers maintain systems and document known limitations
• Leadership sets boundaries for acceptable AI use and escalation protocols
• Clear handoffs between roles prevent gaps in responsibility
• No single role carries the full weight of every decision

Slide 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: When Things Go Wrong

Subtitle: How shared accountability works during problems.

Content:
• Multiple people likely contributed to any AI-related problem
• Focus on understanding the system, not finding someone to blame
• Each role examines their part: procedures, tools, or policies
• Documentation helps trace decision paths across roles
• Learning from incidents strengthens future accountability

Slide 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: Escalation Framework

Subtitle: When and how to raise concerns about AI decisions.

Content:
• Operators escalate when AI outputs seem wrong or uncertain
• Engineers escalate when system limitations affect field decisions
• Leadership escalates when AI use exceeds organizational boundaries
• Clear escalation paths prevent delayed responses to problems
• Speaking up protects everyone when AI recommendations are questionable

Slide 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title: What to Remember

Subtitle: Core principles to carry forward always.

Content:
• Humans stay accountable always—AI suggests, people decide
• Question AI outputs regularly rather than accepting blindly
• Escalate when uncertain—there's no shame in asking for help
• Document every AI-influenced decision for transparency
• Leadership sets the standard that others follow
