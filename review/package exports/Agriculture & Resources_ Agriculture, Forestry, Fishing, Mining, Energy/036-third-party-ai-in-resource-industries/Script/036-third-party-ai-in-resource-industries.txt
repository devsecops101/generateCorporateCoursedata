Welcome to the course. We're going to be exploring something that's becoming increasingly important across resource industries, and that's the risk that comes with third-party AI. Now, when most people think about AI, they imagine a big decision being made to adopt some new technology. But here's the thing: quite often, AI is already arriving in your organisation without anyone making that conscious choice. Your software vendors, your equipment suppliers, your service providers, they're quietly embedding AI tools into the products you already use and pay for. It might be tucked into a contract update or bundled into a routine software upgrade. You may not even notice it's there. And that's precisely where the risk begins. Because when AI comes in through a third party, so do the hidden dependencies, the data-sharing arrangements, and the algorithms you have no direct control over.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's look at where third-party AI typically shows up in resource operations. In drilling and exploration, software platforms increasingly use AI to interpret geological data and optimize drilling patterns. Fleet management systems apply AI to route optimization and fuel efficiency. Procurement platforms use AI to predict supplier performance and pricing trends. Your equipment monitoring systems may use AI to detect patterns that indicate upcoming maintenance needs. Safety reporting tools often incorporate AI to identify risk patterns across incidents. Even seemingly simple services like weather data feeds now include AI-enhanced predictions. The key point is that AI is entering through operational systems you already depend on, not just through new technology purchases.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When we talk about third-party AI, we're really talking about a surprisingly wide range of suppliers. Some are software vendors who build AI directly into tools you might already be using, things like fleet management systems or scheduling platforms. Others are contractors or consultants who turn up on site bringing their own AI tools with them. Then you've got data providers supplying the geological, weather, or market information that feeds into those AI systems, and hardware suppliers who bundle AI into the sensors, cameras, and equipment you're already running. What makes this particularly interesting is that in any single workflow, you might have several of these vendors involved at once, each one depending on another. That chain of dependencies is often completely invisible.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How do you spot AI when vendors don't advertise it? Start by paying attention to the language they use. Terms like 'intelligent automation,' 'smart analytics,' 'predictive insights,' or 'machine learning' are clear indicators. But also watch for more subtle clues. Does the system seem to get better at its job over time without manual updates? Does it provide recommendations that feel sophisticated? Can it detect patterns in large amounts of data that would be impossible to spot manually? These behaviors often signal AI working behind the scenes. Don't forget that marketing materials and user interfaces may downplay the AI component while still benefiting from its capabilities.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Once you suspect AI might be involved, it's time to investigate systematically. Start by asking your suppliers directly whether their services involve AI or machine learning algorithms. Review any recent software updates or service changes that introduced new capabilities. Look closely at how data flows between different systems in your operation. Sometimes AI isn't in the primary tool you're using, but in a secondary system that processes your data. Pay special attention to multi-vendor arrangements where your data might pass through several companies before returning as analysis or recommendations. The goal isn't to eliminate AI, but to understand where it exists so you can manage it appropriately.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Why don't vendors always tell you when they're using AI? Sometimes it's because they consider their AI technology a competitive advantage and don't want to reveal details. Other times, they assume customers care more about results than methods. In complex supply chains, vendors several layers down might be using AI without your direct suppliers even knowing. There's also the gradual rollout factor - AI features may be introduced slowly over time, making each individual change seem minor. And many existing contracts simply weren't written with AI in mind, so there's no obligation to disclose it. Understanding these dynamics helps explain why proactive inquiry is so important.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When you sign up with an AI vendor, you are also signing a contract, and that contract deserves a careful read. Start by checking who actually owns the data you feed into the system. Is it still yours, or does the vendor claim some rights to it? Next, look out for any mention of subcontractors, because your data may be passed along to other AI providers you have never heard of. You will also want to understand who is responsible if the AI makes a costly mistake. Does the liability fall on you, or on the vendor? Have a look for opt-out provisions as well, which let you stop your data being used to train their models. And finally, check whether you have audit rights, meaning the ability to request information about how your data is being handled.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Managing third-party AI isn't a one-time task - it requires ongoing attention. Start by establishing clear policies that require vendors to disclose AI use in their services. Make this a standard part of your procurement process. Regularly review your supplier contracts, especially during renewals, to understand how AI dependencies may have evolved. Train your teams to recognize the signs of AI in vendor services so they can flag potential issues early. Most importantly, avoid becoming overly dependent on any single AI-powered service without understanding how it works and having alternative options. The organizations that stay in control are the ones that ask questions, read the small print, and keep learning as the technology landscape evolves.
