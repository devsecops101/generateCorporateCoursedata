Let us start by thinking about what actually makes an environment "safety-critical" in the first place. The simplest way to put it is this: in some situations, if something goes wrong, people can get hurt or killed. We are not talking about a software bug that causes a minor inconvenience, or a system error that costs a business a bit of money. We are talking about decisions where the stakes are genuinely life and death. Think about medical diagnosis, aircraft control, or industrial machinery operating around people. In those contexts, a mistake does not come with an undo button. You cannot roll back the clock. And what makes AI particularly challenging here is the speed at which these systems operate. Failures can unfold in fractions of a second, leaving no time for a human to step in and correct things. It is not just people at risk either; it is equipment, infrastructure, and entire environments. That is what sets safety-critical AI apart from everything else.

Script 3 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Before a serious incident occurs, there are almost always warning signs. The challenge is that they can be easy to dismiss, especially when things seem to be running smoothly. So what should we be watching for? Think about moments when a sensor suddenly goes quiet, not because everything is fine, but because something has stopped reporting. Or consider those small, low-level alerts that keep appearing and keep getting ignored because they seem minor. Then there are AI outputs that are close to correct but not quite right, which can be just as dangerous as being completely wrong. Pay attention too when staff are regularly overriding system warnings, because that pattern tells you something important about trust in the system. And if your logs are full of frequent human corrections to AI decisions, that is a signal worth investigating. None of these things on their own necessarily means disaster, but together they paint a picture that deserves serious attention.

Script 4 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the most fundamental problems with AI in safety-critical environments is that it can only be as good as the data it receives. In places like mines, oil rigs, or chemical plants, sensors are constantly monitoring things like temperature, pressure, gas levels, and movement. But those sensors operate in incredibly harsh conditions. Dust, heat, and moisture can all cause them to degrade over time, and when a sensor starts giving inaccurate readings, the AI system has absolutely no way of knowing that. It simply trusts what it is told. So if a faulty sensor sends back a false "all clear" signal, the AI will act on that as though everything is perfectly fine. There is no built-in scepticism, no ability to question whether the data makes sense. And in a genuinely dangerous situation, that delay in response could have very serious consequences. The AI is not broken, it is just blind to the problem.

Script 6 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
History has a habit of teaching us the same lessons over and over, often at great cost. In mining, early automated systems sometimes sent false "all clear" signals, and those mistakes directly shaped the safety protocols we still rely on today. Aviation has done something similar, using near-misses to rethink how much we trust automated systems without keeping a human firmly in the loop. And then there is Deepwater Horizon, where automated warnings were dismissed under pressure, with catastrophic results. What these incidents share is a common thread: complexity and speed make it genuinely difficult to catch problems before they escalate. A small error in a normal environment might be an inconvenience. In a safety-critical environment, that same small error can spiral very quickly. So as we look at AI in these high risk settings, we are not starting from scratch. We have history to learn from. The question is whether we are actually listening to it.

Script 7 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Having looked at what can go wrong, let's think about how we actually move forward. The good news is that most failures teach us something valuable, but only if we're willing to act on those lessons. Before any AI system goes live in a safety-critical environment, it needs to be thoroughly tested under realistic conditions, not just ideal ones. Once it's deployed, your staff need regular, ongoing training, because technology changes and people's understanding needs to keep pace with it. When something does go wrong, and at some point it will, the response needs to be honest and open rather than defensive. That means reviewing failures properly and updating systems based on what you find. And perhaps most importantly, don't keep those lessons to yourself. Sharing what you've learned across teams and organisations is how the whole industry gets safer over time. Progress here really is a collective effort.

Script 8 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The most important thing to hold onto is this: when AI gets it wrong in a farm, on a rig, or down a mine, the consequences aren't just a dodgy spreadsheet. People can get hurt. That's why human judgement must always sit alongside any AI output before you act on it. And remember, context matters enormously. An AI system doesn't always understand the full picture the way you do. We also talked about sensor data. If what's going into the system is faulty, the decisions coming out can be quietly dangerous. If you ever notice AI behaving in an unexpected way, report it straight away, even if nothing bad happened this time. Finally, here's a practical starting point. Think of one AI-assisted process in your workplace and ask yourself: who is actually checking what it produces? That single question could make a real difference.
