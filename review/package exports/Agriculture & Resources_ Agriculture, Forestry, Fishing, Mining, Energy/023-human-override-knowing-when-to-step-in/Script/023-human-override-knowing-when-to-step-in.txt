Welcome to this lesson on human override, and specifically, when AI needs your guidance. Now, AI systems are genuinely impressive, but they are not infallible. They make mistakes, sometimes small ones, sometimes significant ones, and quite often they miss the broader context of a situation. They process information, but they do not truly understand it the way you do. That is where you come in. Human oversight is not just a nice extra layer, it is an essential part of working responsibly with AI. Your ability to spot something that feels off, to question an output, or to recognise when a decision carries real consequences, that is something no AI can replicate. Throughout this lesson, we are going to explore how to develop that critical eye, so you know exactly when to step in and take control. Think of yourself not as someone who simply uses AI, but as the safeguard that keeps it on track.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's talk about where AI actually falls short, because this is really important. AI systems learn from historical data, which means when the world changes quickly, they can be slow to catch up. And here's the tricky part: AI doesn't always know when it's wrong. It can give you an answer that sounds completely confident and authoritative, even when the information is outdated or just plain inaccurate. There's also the question of nuance. Things like tone, cultural sensitivity, and context are genuinely difficult for AI to get right. Then there are edge cases, those unusual or unexpected situations that simply don't fit neatly into the patterns the system was trained on. AI tends to struggle with those. And perhaps most importantly, AI has no real understanding of consequences. It doesn't weigh up the impact of what it's suggesting. That's why human judgement isn't just helpful in these moments, it's absolutely essential.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
In resource-based industries like agriculture, mining, and energy, these AI limitations take on particular significance. Weather patterns shift faster than most training data can be updated, but AI might still recommend planting schedules based on last decade's rainfall. Equipment conditions vary dramatically by location, age, and usage, yet AI often assumes standard performance across the board. Safety protocols need human interpretation because they were written by people who understand real-world consequences, not statistical patterns. Environmental factors require local knowledge that no algorithm can capture, and regulatory changes happen in real-time while AI training cycles take months. These aren't abstract problems, they're daily realities that make human oversight absolutely critical in your sector.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do you actually know when it's time to step in? There are a few warning signs worth watching for. One of the most common is when an AI gives you a very confident-sounding answer, but the information it was working with was incomplete or unclear. Confidence doesn't always mean correctness. You should also pay attention if the same type of error keeps cropping up, or if the output contradicts something you know to be true, whether that's a recent event or your organisation's own policies. Sometimes the signal is more subtle, like a sudden shift in tone or logic that doesn't quite make sense. And occasionally, the system itself will flag uncertainty through low confidence scores or incomplete responses, so do take those seriously. Finally, don't dismiss your instincts. If something feels off, that gut feeling is often worth investigating. These signals are your cues to pause and take a closer look.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Beyond general warning signs, there are specific red flags that should trigger immediate human review. If numbers appear in AI output, do a quick sense check, because systems can be confidently wrong about figures that would be obviously incorrect to someone with field experience. Watch for recommendations that seem to ignore established safety protocols or assume perfect operating conditions that rarely exist in real operations. Be particularly cautious when AI advice contradicts recent experience from your team or location, as the system may not have access to that current information. And be suspicious of anything that looks absolutely perfect. Real-world solutions usually involve trade-offs and compromises, so flawless recommendations often signal that the AI has missed something important about the complexity of your situation.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Building confidence in override decisions is genuinely a skill you can develop, and like most skills, it improves with practice. Work through realistic scenarios regularly, the kind where you need to spot when an AI response crosses from useful into potentially risky territory. Practice articulating your concerns out loud or in writing, because this trains your brain to respond with confidence rather than hesitation. When you find yourself hesitating to override a system, take a moment to understand what's holding you back. Often it's not that your instincts are wrong, but that you haven't learned to trust them yet. Remember that your gut feeling is actually your brain processing subtle cues that don't fit into neat categories. That intuition, combined with your experience and knowledge of local conditions, is exactly what makes human oversight so valuable.

Let's work through some realistic scenarios you might encounter. Imagine AI recommends changing equipment settings mid-season based on efficiency data, but you know your particular machinery behaves differently in current field conditions. Or the system suggests proceeding despite weather warnings because historical data shows low risk, but your local weather patterns have shifted recently. Consider an algorithm proposing cost cuts that would affect safety measures, confident in its calculations but unaware of regulatory requirements. What about automated scheduling that conflicts with crew availability or legal work hour limits? These scenarios help you practice identifying the moment when AI confidence meets real-world complexity, and where your expertise becomes essential for safe, effective decisions.

It makes mistakes, and quite often those mistakes are the kind that only a human would notice, because we understand context in a way that AI simply does not.
