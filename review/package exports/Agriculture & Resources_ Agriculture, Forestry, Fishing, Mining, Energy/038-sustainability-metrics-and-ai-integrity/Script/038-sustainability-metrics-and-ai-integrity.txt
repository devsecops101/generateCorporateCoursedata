Welcome to this course on Sustainability Metrics and AI Integrity. We're going to be exploring a topic that is becoming increasingly important for anyone working with ESG data and reporting. Artificial intelligence is now deeply embedded in how organisations collect, analyse, and communicate their sustainability performance. That might sound like progress, and in many ways it is, but it also introduces some serious risks that we need to understand. When AI systems make errors, those errors can ripple through reports and decisions very quickly, often without anyone noticing. If the underlying data has gaps or inconsistencies, the results can be misleading, even when everything looks polished on the surface. This creates real opportunities for greenwashing, where organisations appear more sustainable than they actually are. And when things go wrong, it becomes genuinely difficult to work out who is responsible. So throughout this course, we will look at how to recognise these risks and how to manage them responsibly.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's examine the specific ways AI can fail in ESG reporting. One of the biggest issues is that AI tools can produce figures that sound completely plausible, but when you dig into them, there's no verified source behind the numbers. Many AI systems are trained on data that isn't current, which means the emissions benchmarks they reference could already be out of date. There's also a real greenwashing risk if organisations publish AI-generated content without reviewing it first. Often there's no audit trail, so if someone asks how a particular figure was calculated, you may have no way to answer. Finally, AI tools don't always use consistent terminology, and in a compliance context, that inconsistency can create genuine gaps in your reporting. These specific failure modes are what we need to watch for.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
There's an old saying in computing: garbage in, garbage out. When we feed AI systems poor quality data, we shouldn't be surprised when the outputs are unreliable. If your emissions records have gaps, your AI-generated carbon footprint report will be wrong from the start. If the information about your suppliers is out of date, your scope 3 reporting will be inaccurate. Here's what catches people off guard: errors don't just sit there quietly, they compound. One small mistake early in the process can snowball into something much larger. If a dataset has been mislabelled, the AI might completely misclassify an environmental risk. A tiny input error can produce an ESG conclusion that is wildly misleading. So before we think about what AI can do for sustainability reporting, we need to be honest about the quality of the data we're putting in.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the most important things to understand about AI tools is their knowledge limitations. These systems are trained on data collected at some point in the past, which could be many months or even years before you're using the tool. That means any regulatory updates, revised emissions factors, or new reporting frameworks like TCFD or CSRD may simply not be included, or only partially covered. Here's the tricky part: the AI won't always tell you when it doesn't know something. It will often produce a confident-looking answer even when its information is out of date. Additionally, data covering specific regions or industry sectors tends to be underrepresented, which can quietly skew your results. Understanding these knowledge gaps is crucial before you rely on AI for sustainability reporting.

Script 5 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do you identify when AI knowledge falls short? Start by checking whether recent regulatory changes are reflected in the AI's responses. Verify that emissions factors match the latest standards from recognised bodies. Test any sector-specific calculations against authoritative sources, because niche industries often have poor representation in AI training data. Look out for placeholder data or generic responses when you need precise calculations for complex scenarios. The golden rule is this: always cross-check any figures or guidance the AI gives you against current, authoritative sources before you submit anything. The AI's confidence doesn't guarantee accuracy.

Script 6 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now let's talk about auditing AI-generated ESG data properly. When you receive AI-generated figures, always trace them back to their original sources. Ask yourself: where did this number actually come from? A really useful habit is to place the AI output side by side with the raw source material and compare them directly. You should also look for patterns of repeated errors, because AI tools can make the same mistake consistently, which makes it easy to miss if you're not paying attention. Watch out for gaps or vague language, because AI systems sometimes fill in missing information with plausible-sounding but inaccurate content. And perhaps most importantly, never rely on the algorithm alone. Human reviewers need to be part of this process at every stage.

Script 7 (Slide 7)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Once you have AI-generated sustainability data, the real work begins: making sure you can trust it before it goes anywhere near a report. The first step is manually checking your key figures. Don't just accept a number because the AI produced it. Cross-reference it against verified, reputable data sources to confirm it holds up. If something looks surprising or doesn't fit the pattern you'd expect, treat that as a signal to investigate rather than ignore it. Unexpected results aren't always wrong, but they always deserve a closer look. It's also important to keep a clear record of every output the AI generates, including the prompts you used and when you ran them. That audit trail becomes invaluable if anyone questions your figures later.

Script 8 (Slide 8)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's wrap up with a practical approach to implementing AI responsibly in ESG reporting. If you're just getting started, don't pilot a new AI tool on high-risk official submissions. Begin with something low-risk, like an internal report, so you can build confidence and understand the tool's specific quirks before the stakes are higher. Use this pilot phase to develop robust verification processes that you'll need later. Train your team on both the capabilities and limitations of whatever AI tools you're using. Establish clear documentation standards so everyone knows how to maintain audit trails. And remember: human oversight is non-negotiable when data informs regulatory or investor disclosures. These principles aren't complicated, but they do require consistency and discipline every single time you use AI in your sustainability workflow.
