So let's get to the heart of what this course is really about. The core problem with using AI in publishing is this: AI tools can produce content that sounds completely authoritative and well-researched, but is factually wrong. And here's what makes that particularly tricky. The AI itself has no way of checking whether what it's written is accurate. It simply generates text that sounds plausible. For publishers, that creates a real risk. If that unverified content goes out to your audience, you're potentially spreading misinformation at scale. And because AI-generated errors tend to look polished and professional, they don't have the obvious hallmarks of a mistake. They can slip right past a busy editor. That risk multiplies when content is moving quickly through your pipeline, with little time built in for proper review. So the question we're exploring throughout this course is: how do you work with AI responsibly, and catch those errors before they reach your readers?

So how do you actually spot AI-generated misinformation before it makes its way into something you publish? There are a few telltale signs worth keeping in mind. One of the most common is the use of statistics that sound very precise and authoritative, but when you try to track them down, they simply don't exist. Alongside that, you'll often notice the writing has a very confident, assured tone, yet when you look closely, there's very little solid evidence backing up the claims. You might also find there are no named sources at all, just vague references to "studies" or "experts" without any specifics. Another red flag is when dates or facts within the same piece start to contradict each other. And finally, watch out for quotes that feel oddly generic, with no clear attribution to a real person. These patterns won't always appear together, but spotting even one or two of them should prompt you to investigate further before publishing.

So let's talk about what AI errors actually look like in the real world, because they can be surprisingly easy to miss. One of the most common issues is statistics. AI will confidently produce a figure that sounds completely reasonable, but when you go looking for the original source, it simply does not exist. Names and dates are another weak spot, often slightly wrong in ways that are easy to overlook if you are moving quickly. You should also be cautious with quotes. If a quote attributed to a well-known person feels a little vague or just slightly out of character, that is worth investigating. The good news is that catching these errors does not have to be complicated. Simply checking one claim against two or three reliable sources will flag most problems fairly quickly. And perhaps the most important thing to remember is this: just because AI writes something in a confident, authoritative tone does not mean it is accurate.

So let's bring this all together with the things I really want you to hold onto as you move forward. AI tools can be genuinely useful in publishing, but they are not infallible, and they are certainly not a replacement for human judgement. Every single thing an AI produces needs to be checked by a real person, and that person is you. Facts, figures, names, dates, sources, all of it needs to be verified before it goes anywhere near a reader. And please do take sources seriously, because if an AI cites something, you need to confirm that source actually exists and says what the AI claims it says. This matters because mistakes travel incredibly quickly online, and once something inaccurate is published, it can be very difficult to undo the damage. So think of yourself as the final filter in this process. The technology can assist, but the responsibility always sits with you.
