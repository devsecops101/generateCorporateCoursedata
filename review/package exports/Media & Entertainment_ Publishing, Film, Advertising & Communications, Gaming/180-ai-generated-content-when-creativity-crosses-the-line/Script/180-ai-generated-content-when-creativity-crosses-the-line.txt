Let's start with something that might surprise you. AI tools can be incredibly impressive, but they are not infallible. They make mistakes, and sometimes those mistakes look completely convincing. The real problem is how quickly that content spreads. Someone shares something online, others assume it is accurate, and before long a piece of AI-generated misinformation has reached thousands of people. And it is not just a small group of tech-savvy users who are at risk here. Anyone can be misled, regardless of age, education, or experience. What makes this even trickier is that the quality of AI-generated content varies enormously. Some of it is genuinely excellent, and some of it is riddled with errors, and the two can look almost identical at first glance. So the stakes here are real. Whether we are talking about health information, financial advice, or news stories, getting it wrong can have serious consequences. That is exactly what this lesson is here to help you navigate.

So before we go any further, let's just take a moment to look at the bigger picture of what can go wrong when AI starts generating content. And honestly, the list is longer than most people expect. One of the trickiest issues is that AI can sound incredibly convincing even when it's completely wrong. It doesn't hesitate, it doesn't second-guess itself, it just confidently produces nonsense. On top of that, these tools are trained on human-generated data, which means any bias that existed in that data can quietly sneak into the output. There's also the sheer scale to consider. One person with an AI tool can flood the internet with misleading content in minutes. Offensive or inappropriate material can appear without much warning, and the quality of what you get depends heavily on how well you phrase your request. When misinformation spreads at this speed, real people can be genuinely harmed. That's what makes this worth taking seriously.

Script 3 (Slide 3)

One of the trickiest things about AI-generated content is that it can sound completely convincing, even when it is completely wrong. AI tools can fabricate facts out of thin air, getting dates, names, and figures muddled up, and yet they present all of that in a confident, authoritative tone. There is no hesitation, no "I am not sure about this one." It just states things as though they are true. And here is where it gets a bit more subtle: AI can also carry hidden bias, reflecting skewed perspectives from the data it was trained on, without you even noticing. Now, combine that with how quickly content spreads online, and you can see why this matters. A single piece of inaccurate AI content can reach thousands of people before anyone spots the problem. So before you publish or share anything created with AI, it is absolutely worth taking a moment to verify the details yourself.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do you actually spot AI-generated content that isn't up to scratch? There are a few telltale signs to watch out for. One of the most common is repetition, where the text seems to circle back and restate the same point in slightly different words. You might also notice confident-sounding statements that don't actually say very much, with no real evidence or detail to back them up. Factual errors are another red flag, and these can be surprisingly easy to miss if you're not checking carefully. Sometimes the tone just feels a little off, almost too formal or stiff, like nobody quite talks that way in real life. Generic examples are worth watching for too, the kind that could slot into absolutely any article on any subject. And finally, keep an eye out for sudden changes in quality or style within the same piece of writing. Once you know what to look for, these patterns become much easier to recognise.

Script 7 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
When your organisation publishes content created with AI, your brand is the one standing behind it. The tool that generated it won't be held accountable if something goes wrong. You will. And it only takes one poorly worded, misleading, or offensive piece of content to seriously damage the trust you've spent years building with your audience. That's why it's so important to review everything before it goes out, whether that's a social media post, a customer email, or a report. Don't assume that because AI produced it, it's ready to publish. You need to treat AI-generated content with exactly the same level of care and scrutiny you'd apply to anything else that goes out under your name. It's also worth setting clear internal standards so that everyone in your team understands what is and isn't acceptable to publish. Think of it as protecting something genuinely valuable: the reputation you've worked hard to earn.

Script 8 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So as we wrap up, let me leave you with a few things worth holding onto. First, never just accept what the AI gives you at face value. Always check it, question it, verify it. Second, remember that bias does not announce itself. It can slip into AI output quietly, so you need to stay alert to it. Third, quality does not look after itself. A human eye and a human judgement are still essential parts of the process. Fourth, think about context. What works in one situation may be completely inappropriate in another, and you are the one who understands the nuances of your audience and your purpose. And finally, you are the one setting the standard. The AI is a tool, and like any tool, the results depend on the person using it. You have the responsibility, and honestly, that is a good thing. Keep these principles in mind and you will be in a much stronger position to use AI content wisely.
