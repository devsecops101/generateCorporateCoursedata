Welcome, and thank you for joining this course. What we're going to explore together is genuinely important, and in some ways quite sobering. Artificial intelligence is no longer just a topic for tech conferences or science fiction films. It is already embedded in the systems that move us around every day, from the aircraft we fly in, to the trains we commute on, to the cars increasingly driving themselves on our roads. And here is the thing: when AI makes a mistake in those environments, the consequences are not just inconvenient. They can be fatal. At the same time, the pace at which AI is being adopted in transport is accelerating, which means the risks are growing faster than many people realise. This course is designed to help you understand what those risks actually look like, where they come from, and why they matter. You do not need a technical background to follow along. You just need to be curious and willing to engage with some genuinely critical questions.

Let us start by getting clear on what we actually mean when we talk about safety-critical AI systems. These are systems where, if something goes wrong, the consequences are not just an inconvenience or a minor setback. We are talking about real potential for serious injury, loss of life, or large-scale disaster. Think about aircraft autopilots keeping hundreds of passengers in the air, railway signalling systems coordinating trains across a busy network, or autonomous vehicles navigating public roads. In all of these environments, the reliability standards are extraordinarily high, far beyond anything we would expect from, say, a smartphone app or a recommendation algorithm. And here is the crucial point: even a very rare error, something that might only happen once in a million operations, can still be catastrophic and completely irreversible. There is simply no room for "close enough" or "good enough" in these contexts. The bar is exceptionally high, and for very good reason.

What actually goes wrong when AI makes a mistake in a safety-critical environment, because the consequences here are very different from, say, a spelling error in a word processor. When an AI system misreads sensor data, perhaps confusing a pedestrian for a shadow, or misjudging the distance to the vehicle ahead, that error can translate directly into a real-world collision. And it is not just about wrong decisions in the moment. Sometimes the problem is a delayed response, where the system takes a fraction of a second too long to react, and in transport, fractions of seconds matter enormously. There is also the issue of false signals, where the AI overrides a human operator who actually had the right instinct. What makes all of this so serious is that errors which might seem tiny at the technical level can escalate incredibly quickly into catastrophic outcomes. That is the core challenge we are exploring throughout this course.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sensors are absolutely fundamental to how AI operates in transport systems. Sensors are the AI's eyes and ears. They measure things like speed, distance, and whether there's an obstacle ahead. The problem is, the AI has no way of questioning what the sensors tell it. If a sensor sends faulty data, the AI simply acts on it, instantly and without hesitation. Think of it like giving someone wrong directions and then wondering why they ended up in the wrong place. The AI isn't being careless, it just trusts its inputs completely. And there are plenty of real-world reasons sensors can go wrong. Rain, fog, mud, even a bit of road grime can interfere with readings. A dirty camera or a blocked radar sensor can cause the AI to misread a situation entirely. So when we say bad input causes bad decisions, that's not a criticism of the AI itself. It's a reminder that the hardware feeding it information has to be reliable too.

These examples aren't just historical footnotes, they're warnings we should take seriously. In 2009, Air France Flight 447 crashed into the Atlantic after the crew became confused by conflicting automated systems, triggered by faulty sensors. The Therac-25 radiation machine is another sobering case, where a tiny software bug removed critical safety limits and patients died as a result. More recently, the Boeing 737 MAX MCAS system repeatedly overrode pilot inputs because it was over-relying on a single sensor, contributing to two fatal crashes. What's striking when you look across all of these incidents is the common pattern running through them. In each case, human oversight was reduced or undermined at precisely the moment it was most needed. A small fault, a confused sensor, a race condition in code, these things can cascade very quickly into catastrophic outcomes that simply cannot be undone. That's why understanding where AI and automation can fail is so important.

Script 8 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring everything together from this lesson. When AI is used in transport systems, the risks are genuinely high. Mistakes are not just inconvenient, they can be fatal, so we have to get this right. That means thoroughly testing any system before it ever goes near real passengers or infrastructure. It also means keeping human beings firmly in the loop, because AI should support decisions, not replace human judgement entirely. The quality of the data you feed into these systems matters enormously too. Rubbish data in, dangerous decisions out. Once a system is live, you cannot simply walk away. Regular audits help you catch problems before they become disasters. And perhaps most importantly, when something does go wrong, treat it as a learning opportunity. Every failure carries a lesson that can make the next system safer. These are the principles that should guide anyone working with AI in safety-critical environments.
