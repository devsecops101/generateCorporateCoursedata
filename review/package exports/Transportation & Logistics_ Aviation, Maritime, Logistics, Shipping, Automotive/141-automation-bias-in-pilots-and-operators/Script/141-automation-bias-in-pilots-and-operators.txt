Welcome to this course on automation bias in pilots and operators. To get us started, let's think about what modern cockpits actually look like today. They are filled with sophisticated automated systems that handle everything from navigation to alerts and flight control. And because these systems are so capable and so reliable, it is completely natural for pilots to place a great deal of trust in them. But here is where things get interesting. When that trust starts to tip over into over-reliance, when a pilot begins to favour what the automation is telling them over their own judgement and training, we enter the territory of automation bias. And even a small shift in that balance of trust can lead to significant errors in decision-making. What makes this particularly important is that automation bias does not just affect inexperienced pilots. It affects seasoned professionals too, as well as operators across the wider aviation industry and beyond. So let us explore exactly what that means and why it matters.

Script 3 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, what exactly is automation bias? In simple terms, it's what happens when we start to trust a machine's judgement more than our own. And this happens more often than you might think. When an automated system tells us everything is fine, we tend to believe it, even if something in our gut, or right in front of us, is suggesting otherwise. We might ignore a warning light or override our own instincts simply because the system hasn't flagged a problem. Over time, this creates a really dangerous habit where we stop actively monitoring what's going on around us. We become passive. We're just watching the automation do its job rather than staying mentally engaged ourselves. This is particularly common in cockpits and industrial control rooms, where operators are surrounded by sophisticated technology. And the scary part is that when things do go wrong, they can go wrong very quickly indeed.

Script 4 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So how do you actually know when automation bias is starting to creep in? There are some tell-tale warning signs to watch for. One of the most common is when pilots or operators begin dismissing unexpected alerts, perhaps assuming the system has made an error rather than stopping to investigate. Alongside that, you might notice people skipping the manual cross-checks they would normally carry out, simply because the automation appears to be handling everything. There is also a tendency to trust what a display is showing without independently verifying whether that information is actually correct. Over time, these habits quietly chip away at your overall situational awareness, meaning you have a less accurate picture of what is really happening around you. And perhaps most dangerously, when something does go wrong, the response can be noticeably slower because the operator has mentally handed control over to the system. Recognising these patterns early is absolutely essential.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
So, what can we actually do about automation bias? The good news is that awareness is already a strong first step. Pilots and operators should make a point of practising manual skills regularly, so those abilities stay sharp when automation isn't available or fails unexpectedly. When a system alert appears, treat it as genuinely important rather than something to dismiss. It's also worth getting into the habit of checking automated outputs against other sources, rather than simply accepting what the system tells you. The shift from passive watching to active monitoring is a small but powerful change in mindset. And importantly, it's absolutely fine to question what the system is telling you, particularly in unusual or degraded situations. At an organisational level, training programmes should help crews spot the early warning signs of over-reliance before it becomes a problem. These aren't complicated changes, but practised consistently, they can make a real difference to safety.

Script 8 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's bring everything together with a few essential reminders to carry forward. First, never rely solely on what the automation is telling you. Use your own situational awareness to sense-check what you're seeing on screen. Your training exists for a reason, so trust it. Remember that automation can fail quietly, with no alarm and no obvious sign that anything is wrong, which is precisely what makes it so dangerous. To guard against over-reliance, keep your manual skills sharp by practising them regularly. If something the system does doesn't quite add up, question it rather than assuming the machine must be right. And finally, make sure you're familiar with your organisation's guidance on how automation should be used. If you have concerns, raise them early rather than waiting for a problem to develop. These points aren't complicated, but keeping them in mind genuinely makes a difference to how safely you operate in an automated environment.
