So here's something that might surprise you. An AI tool can be right nineteen times out of twenty and still be genuinely dangerous in a financial context. Ninety-five percent accuracy sounds impressive, but think about what that remaining five percent actually means in practice. If a system miscalculates a loan figure, or gets a tax liability wrong, that single error can lead to real financial harm for a real person. And in finance, mistakes rarely stay isolated. One wrong number can feed into another calculation, and another, and the problem grows quickly. There's also a subtler issue here. A high overall accuracy score can hide the fact that the errors are clustering in exactly the situations where precision matters most. So the question we need to ask about any AI tool isn't simply "how often is it right?" It's "what happens when it's wrong, and can we afford that?"

Now, here's where things get really important. In finance, even a tiny error can have enormous consequences. A model that's ninety-nine percent accurate sounds impressive, but if that one percent miss means a fraudulent transaction slips through, real money is gone and it doesn't come back. Or imagine a system recommending an interest rate that's just slightly off. That small miscalculation could lead a business to take on a loan it can't afford, or pass up an investment it should have taken. And here's the tricky part: AI systems often present their answers with a great deal of confidence, which can actually make things worse. When a system sounds certain, people are less likely to question it. So the gap between what the model thinks it knows and what it actually knows stays hidden. "Close enough" might be fine when you're estimating how long dinner will take, but in financial decisions, that gap can be genuinely costly.

One of the trickiest problems with AI in financial decisions is not when it gets something wrong outright. It is when it gets something partially right, but stays quiet about the parts it is unsure of. That silence is actually a signal. It often means the model has gaps in its knowledge that it is not flagging for you. And here is the really dangerous bit: those gaps can hide inside answers that look completely correct on the surface. You might see a confident response, maybe even a confidence score of 90 percent or higher, and still be missing crucial context that changes the entire picture. That missing context has a real cost, whether that is a mispriced risk, a missed obligation, or a decision made on incomplete information. The hardest situations to manage are the ones where you do not even know what you do not know. Those hidden blind spots tend to cause the most damage in practice.

Script 7 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How do we actually reduce the risk of AI errors causing real financial harm? A few practical habits make a big difference. First, never act on a single AI-generated figure without checking it against another source. If the numbers matter, verify them. Second, for any significant financial decision, build in a human review step. Set a threshold and make sure a person signs off above it. It is also worth keeping clear records of where AI was involved in a process and what assumptions the tool was working from. That kind of documentation protects you if questions arise later. You should also test your AI tools periodically using data where you already know the correct answer, because accuracy can quietly degrade over time without any obvious warning signs. And perhaps most importantly, always treat AI output as a starting point for your thinking, not the final word. That mindset alone will save you from a lot of costly mistakes.

The core message of this lesson is that when AI gets something wrong in a financial context, there are real consequences, whether that's a poor investment decision, a compliance failure, or a missed risk. And here's the thing: an AI tool can be highly accurate in general and still give you the wrong answer for your specific situation. Context matters enormously. The same output can be perfectly reasonable in one scenario and completely misleading in another. That's why verification isn't optional, especially when the stakes are high. Before you act on any critical AI output, check it, question it, and sense-check it against what you already know. And perhaps most importantly, remember that the responsibility never transfers to the machine. You are the decision-maker. AI is there to support your thinking, not to replace your judgement. Keep those principles in mind and you'll be in a much stronger position going forward.
