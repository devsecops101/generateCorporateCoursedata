Welcome to AI in Credit Scoring and Lending Risks. Over the next few lessons, we are going to explore one of the most significant shifts happening in financial services right now. For decades, deciding whether to approve a loan meant a person sitting down, reviewing your application, checking your credit history, and making a judgement call. That process was slow, inconsistent, and often quite subjective. Today, artificial intelligence is changing all of that. Lenders are now using AI systems to assess applicants automatically, drawing on vast amounts of data to make faster, more consistent decisions. On the surface, that sounds like a straightforward improvement. But as we will discover, it also raises some genuinely important questions around fairness, bias, and who gets left behind. Regulators around the world, including here in the UK, are paying close attention. So let us start from the beginning and build up a clear picture of how this all works.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let us start by understanding what we actually mean when we talk about AI in credit scoring. At its core, AI is about finding patterns in large amounts of data, patterns that a human analyst simply could not spot quickly or consistently on their own. In the context of lending, that means an AI system can look at a borrower's financial history, behaviour, and other relevant information, and then predict how likely that person is to repay a loan. Traditionally, this kind of assessment was done manually, which was slow and often inconsistent. AI changes that by learning from thousands or even millions of past lending decisions, using that historical data to get smarter over time. The result is a system that can automatically approve or decline a loan application in a matter of seconds. It is faster, more scalable, and, as we will explore throughout this course, it comes with some important questions we need to think carefully about.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How does AI actually score a borrower? Well, think about what a traditional credit check looks at. It is mostly your payment history, how much you owe, and how long you have had credit. Useful, yes, but quite limited. AI goes much further than that. It can analyse thousands of data points at once, things that a human analyst simply would not have the time or capacity to spot patterns in. And importantly, it is not a one-time snapshot. AI models update scores in real time as new information comes in, so the picture of a borrower stays current rather than going stale. It also looks beyond just whether someone has paid their bills on time. It considers a much broader range of signals to build a more complete financial profile. And the more borrower data it processes, the better it gets at making those assessments. It is genuinely a step change from the old way of doing things.

Script 4 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Here's where things get a little uncomfortable. When an AI makes a credit decision, it's processing millions of data points all at once, and there's no neat, logical trail you can follow to understand why it said yes or no. Traditional credit scoring had rules you could point to. AI doesn't work that way. It learns patterns from enormous amounts of historical data, but it can't then turn around and explain those patterns in plain English. There's no simple "because your income was too low" or "because you missed three payments." The reasoning just doesn't exist in a form that humans can easily read. And that's the core of what we call the black box problem. The model produces an output, but the journey from input to output is essentially invisible. For lenders, regulators, and borrowers alike, that's a real challenge, because decisions that affect people's financial lives really do need to be explainable.

Script 6 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now, whenever we talk about AI in lending, we cannot ignore the regulatory landscape sitting behind it all. There are several important frameworks shaping how lenders are allowed to use AI, including the EU AI Act, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act in the United States, and here in the UK, the Financial Conduct Authority's own fairness guidelines. What these regulations have in common is a clear expectation that lenders can actually explain how their AI models are making decisions. It is not enough to say "the algorithm decided." Regulators want to see the reasoning. They also expect firms to actively test their systems for bias before those systems go anywhere near real customers. And importantly, compliance here is not a box-ticking exercise you can skip. Lenders need to maintain proper audit trails and model governance records, so that if a regulator comes knocking, you can demonstrate accountability clearly and confidently. Transparency and documentation are absolutely central to getting this right.

Script 8 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The summary is this: AI in lending is powerful, but it carries real responsibility. These models need to be audited regularly, because bias can creep in without anyone noticing, and that has genuine consequences for real people. Explainability is absolutely essential, not a nice-to-have. Regulators here in the UK, and increasingly around the world, expect lenders to be able to explain automated decisions in plain, straightforward language. The quality of your training data matters enormously too. If it does not reflect the full diversity of your applicants, your model will reflect those gaps in ways that can be genuinely harmful. And always ask what your model is missing, not just what it captures. As a practical next step, I'd encourage you to look at your own organisation's lending processes and ask honestly where greater transparency or human oversight might be needed. That reflection is where real change begins.
