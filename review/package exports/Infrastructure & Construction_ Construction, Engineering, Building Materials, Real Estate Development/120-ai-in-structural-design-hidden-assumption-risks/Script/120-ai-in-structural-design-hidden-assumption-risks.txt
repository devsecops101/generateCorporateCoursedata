Welcome to this course on AI in structural design, and specifically the hidden assumption risks that come with it. Now, AI tools are genuinely impressive. They can process enormous amounts of data and generate design outputs incredibly quickly. But AI does not always know what it does not know. It can quietly skip over constraints that an experienced engineer would instinctively recognise, and those gaps in understanding can lead to real, serious failures in the physical world. We have already seen cases where AI-generated outputs looked perfectly reasonable on screen but contained assumptions that simply did not hold up in practice. So throughout this course, the message is not that AI is bad or that you should avoid it. Quite the opposite. The message is that you need to stay in the loop. Use these tools, absolutely, but always verify what they produce. Your engineering judgement is not optional. It is the safety net that makes AI actually useful.

Script 2 (Slide 2)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
So let's start by understanding what we actually mean by hidden assumption risks. When you use an AI tool in structural design, it doesn't always tell you what it's assuming behind the scenes. If there's a gap in the information you've provided, the AI will quietly fill it in using whatever defaults it has been trained on. It might assume perfectly standard load conditions, for example, or ignore constraints that are completely specific to your site. The trouble is, those defaults were built for a general case, not your particular project. So the AI treats them as universal truths, even when they aren't. And because none of this is flagged up explicitly, the missing context simply goes unnoticed. You get an output that looks perfectly reasonable, but it's built on assumptions you never agreed to and may not even be aware of. That's the core risk we're going to be exploring throughout this lesson.

Script 3 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The real danger with AI tools in structural design is not what they get wrong openly, it is what they assume quietly. These systems are trained on historical project data, which means old load standards and outdated practices can be baked right in, with no warning label attached. If you are working to a regional code that differs from what the model was trained on, it will not flag that up. It will simply carry on. The same goes for material properties, where the algorithm will typically assume everything is perfectly uniform unless you specifically tell it otherwise. Boundary conditions, whether a joint is fixed, pinned, or free, are often defaulted without the engineer even being asked. And underneath all of this, many tools are quietly optimising for cost and weight reduction, not for the safety margins we actually care about. These are invisible decisions, and that is precisely what makes them so risky.

Script 5 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Load path is one of those concepts that sounds straightforward but catches people out constantly, even experienced engineers. The question is simple: where do the forces actually travel through the structure? AI tools can get this wrong because they sometimes assume forces follow the most obvious or direct route, rather than the real structural path. And when that assumption is off, stress ends up in members that were never designed to handle it. Connections are usually the first things to suffer, because they sit right at the junction of those misunderstood paths. What makes this particularly tricky is that even a small geometry change, something as seemingly minor as an offset beam, can completely reroute how forces move through a structure. The concern is that an AI tool may not raise a flag when a proposed layout quietly contradicts the intended load distribution. So always verify the load path yourself, and do not rely on the tool to spot that conflict for you.

Script 6 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Once you have an AI-generated design output in front of you, the real work begins. Before anything moves forward, always run your load calculations manually. Do not simply trust the numbers the tool has produced. From there, double-check that the material specifications actually match what is available and appropriate for your project. It sounds obvious, but AI tools can suggest materials that are technically valid in general but wrong for your specific context. You also want to cross-reference everything against the relevant building codes, because the AI may be drawing on guidance from a different region or an outdated standard. If the geometry looks unusual or unexpected, that is a signal to stop and question it rather than assume the tool knows best. And finally, connection details should always be verified independently. These are the points where structures can fail, so they deserve your full attention. Validation is not optional; it is simply part of the process.

Script 7 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
We know the risks are real. The question is, what do we actually do about them? The good news is there are some straightforward steps you can put in place. First, never take AI outputs at face value. Always check them against recognised structural codes and standards, and manually verify any load assumptions the tool has made. Crucially, bring a qualified structural engineer into the process at every review stage, not just at the end when it may be too late to catch something. Make sure every assumption the AI has used is written down and visible, because hidden assumptions are where things go wrong. It is also worth running sensitivity checks, testing what happens if material strength or load distribution varies slightly. And finally, build in a specific review step to consider site conditions that the AI simply may not have had access to. These steps together give you a much stronger safety net.

The core message is this: AI tools are genuinely useful in structural design, but they do not automatically know about the hidden constraints that you, as the engineer on the ground, are aware of. That means every output AI produces needs your eyes on it before anything moves forward. And please do not assume that a calculation is correct simply because it looks polished or confident. Your local building codes and regulations always take priority, and no AI system will know those as well as you do. Your professional judgement is not optional here, it is absolutely essential. Every single time you use an AI tool, make a habit of questioning the assumptions baked into its results. Check the assumptions, check the constraints, and see what you find. You might be surprised.
