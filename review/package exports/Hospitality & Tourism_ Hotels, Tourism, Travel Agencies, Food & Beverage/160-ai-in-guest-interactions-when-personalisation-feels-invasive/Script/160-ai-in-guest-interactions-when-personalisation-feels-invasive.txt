Thanks for joining this lesson. We're going to be exploring one of the more nuanced sides of AI in hospitality, and that's the tension between personalisation and privacy. Now, AI gives us some genuinely impressive tools. It can remember a guest's room preferences, their dietary requirements, even the little details from a previous stay, and use all of that to create an experience that feels thoughtful and tailored. And that's brilliant, when it works well. But here's the thing: guests aren't always aware of what information is being collected, or how it's being used behind the scenes. And that gap in understanding can cause real discomfort. What feels like attentive service to one person can feel unsettling or even intrusive to another. So throughout this lesson, we're going to look at where that line sits, why it matters, and how you can make sure your use of AI keeps guests feeling valued rather than watched. Let's get into it.

Here's something interesting about personalisation in hospitality. Guests genuinely want to feel recognised and looked after, but there's a point where that same attentiveness starts to feel a little uncomfortable. And that line is much thinner than most people expect. Think about it this way: a receptionist greeting you by name feels warm and welcoming. But if a staff member mentions, unprompted, that they've noted your dietary preferences from three visits ago, that can feel quite different. Suddenly you're wondering, what else do they know? Now, all of this personalisation is powered by data that hotels collect over time, and that data can be genuinely useful. The tricky part is that every guest is different. What one person finds delightful, another finds unsettling, depending on their background and expectations. So comfort and concern aren't opposites here. They can actually exist side by side in the very same interaction. That's the paradox we're going to be exploring throughout this lesson.

Script 4 (Slide 3)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Let's talk about where the line actually sits between personalisation that feels helpful and personalisation that feels intrusive. It really comes down to consent and transparency. Before you collect any guest data, ask for permission. Don't assume that because someone booked a room, they've agreed to everything. And when you explain what you're doing with their information, be straightforward about it. Plain language, not clever marketing speak. It's also worth remembering that silence is not agreement. If a guest hasn't actively opted in, you can't treat that as a green light. Be clear about what their data is actually used for, whether that's remembering their pillow preference or tailoring future offers. And importantly, if they want out, make that easy. A buried unsubscribe link or a confusing settings page sends entirely the wrong message. When guests feel informed and in control, trust grows. When they feel tricked or trapped, it disappears very quickly.

Script 5 (Slide 4)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
How do we actually know whether a guest is comfortable with personalisation or not? Well, guests give us signals all the time, we just need to pay attention. When someone freely volunteers information, telling you their preferences, their dietary needs, their favourite table, that is a green light. They are inviting you to use that knowledge. But if a guest gives short answers, changes the subject, or seems a little guarded, that is worth noticing. Body language matters too. Crossed arms, avoiding eye contact, a slightly awkward pause can all tell you something words do not. On the other hand, a guest who keeps coming back and behaves consistently is usually telling you they are happy with how you are handling their information. And if someone looks genuinely surprised when you reference something personal about them, that is a sign they did not expect you to know that. In those moments, it is better to dial back rather than push forward.

Script 6 (Slide 5)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Now, here is something that might seem counterintuitive at first. Giving guests control over their own data actually works in your favour. When you ask people to opt in, rather than quietly collecting everything by default, something really positive happens. Guests feel respected, and that feeling matters enormously in hospitality. They are more likely to share accurate information when they choose to do so willingly, because they understand why you are asking and what benefit they will receive in return. You also end up with far fewer complaints about privacy, because there is no sense of being watched or caught off guard. And here is the really important part: when guests trust you with their preferences, that trust tends to grow over time. It becomes the foundation of genuine loyalty, not just repeat bookings driven by habit or price. Transparency is not a barrier to personalisation. It is actually what makes personalisation feel welcome rather than intrusive.

Script 7 (Slide 6)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
We've talked about what personalisation can do well, but now let's look at where it starts to go wrong. Guests are perceptive. When a system seems to know something they never actually told anyone, it can feel unsettling rather than impressive. And that's the tipping point we need to understand. Inferring preferences from behaviour, things like browsing habits or past choices, can seem clever in the background, but the moment a guest becomes aware of it, it can shift from feeling like attentive service to feeling like surveillance. It's not just about what you know, it's about how and when you use it. The same suggestion can feel genuinely helpful or deeply intrusive depending on the timing, the tone, and the context in which it's delivered. And here's the part that is really important: trust is slow to build and quick to lose. One misjudged interaction can undo the goodwill of many previous visits.

So as we wrap up, let me leave you with the things that really matter here. Personalisation is powerful, but it only works well when you set clear limits around what you collect and how you use it. Guests are far more likely to trust you when they feel they have a genuine choice, so always make consent part of the process. And remember, you do not need mountains of data to create a great experience. Often, less is more, and that approach actually shows more respect for your guests. Being open and honest about how your AI tools work will also go a long way towards making people feel comfortable rather than watched. Finally, this is not something you set up once and forget about. Your AI settings and policies need regular reviews to make sure they still reflect what your guests actually expect. Keep these principles close, and you will be in a much stronger position moving forward.
